# BURNWILLOW

## The Sourcebook

### A Tabletop Roleplaying Game of Gear, Decay, and the Last Warm Light

Version 1.0 | 2026

---

## What Is This Game?

Burnwillow is a tabletop roleplaying game for 2-5 people. One person is the **Game Master (GM)** -- they describe the world, play the NPCs, and run the enemies. Everyone else plays **characters** -- survivors in a dying world-tree, delving into its rotting roots to scavenge the gear that keeps them alive.

There are no classes. There are no levels. **You are what you wear.** A rusted shortsword and a leather jerkin make you a scrapper. A moonstone staff and silken robes make you a sorcerer. Swap the gear and you swap the identity. Your character's power comes entirely from what they find, craft, and equip.

Death is permanent. When your character falls, they become a Hollow -- a mindless husk that haunts the dungeon. What survives is legacy: Memory Seeds that unlock crafting recipes for the next character, town upgrades that benefit the whole settlement, and the stories told around the hearth about the ones who didn't come back.

### What You Need to Play

- **This book.** The GM keeps it open to the relevant tables.
- **5d6 per player.** Different colors help visualize the dice pool.
- **Character sheets.** One per player -- the GearGrid with 10 equipment slots, inventory, stats, HP, and a Doom tracker. (A blank sheet template is provided in Appendix D.)
- **Pencils.** You will erase a lot. Characters die. Gear breaks. The Doom Clock advances.

That is all. Everything else -- amber, ingredients, faction reputation, quest progress -- is tracked on the character sheet or the GM's notes.

## How to Use This Book

This sourcebook is divided into eight parts.

**Parts I through III** are for players: the world, character creation, and rules. Read these and stop. Everything beyond that wall contains secrets your character has not earned.

**Parts IV through VIII** are for the Game Master: the setting gazetteer, bestiary, treasure tables, procedural generation tools, and campaign guidance.

If you are a GM, read everything. Then read it again. The connections between factions, NPCs, and zones are load-bearing -- pull one thread and the whole tapestry shifts.

Cross-references appear throughout as "(see Chapter X)." When a rule or concept is explained in detail elsewhere, we point you there rather than repeating ourselves. The Burnwillow has enough repetition in its dying corridors. The book does not need more.

## Chapter 1: The Living Cosmos

### The Six Groves

The Burnwillow universe is a single living cosmos. At its heart are six Groves: four Seasonal world-trees of planetary scale, one shared root network beneath them all, and one hidden origin-trunk accessible only by going inward through the wood itself. The canopy is the sky. The roots are the underworld. There is nothing else. The trees are dying. The Rot is winning. The players are the last ones trying to stop it.

The Seasonal Groves are where people live. The Undergrove is where everything ends up. The Heartwood is where everything began. The Root-Roads connect them -- surface to surface, surface to deep, and if you go far enough inward, surface to origin.

#### The Seasonal Groves

The four seasonal Groves are world-trees of planetary scale, each embodying a season and a philosophical state. These are not metaphors -- the seasons are real, structural properties of each tree's biology and the civilizations it supports. The canopy of each Grove is its entire sky. The Deep Shade beyond the reach of each canopy's fire-light is a frozen, pitch-black expanse where no life can survive.

#### Burnwillow -- Autumn (Active Game Zone)

The dying hearth. Fire-leaves in perpetual crimson, orange, and gold -- the only source of warmth and light in existence. Amber sap still flows in the upper veins, but the deep roots are compromised. Emberhome clings to the mid-trunk -- a village of scavengers and survivors built directly into the petrified, stone-like bark. The Blight rose first here. The Burnwillow is the front line.

The tree physically thrashes in pain from the Rot -- crushing old tunnels, tearing open new ones. This is why dungeons are procedural: geography is genuinely unstable. Every run through the same zone produces different corridors, different rooms, different threats. The map you drew yesterday is wrong today.

**Atmosphere:** Woodsmoke, petrified bark corridors, amber-lit chambers, silence broken by distant fungal growth sounds.

**Theme:** Harvest and decay. What you gather from the dying tree to survive another day. Scarcity, sacrifice, the warmth of community against encroaching darkness.

#### Verdhollow -- Spring (Drowning in Growth)

Uncontrolled growth. Everything blooms too fast, too wild. Roots split stone overnight, vines strangle paths between visits, new life smothers old. The Blight here wears green -- indistinguishable from natural growth until it is too late. The druids of Verdhollow are not tending a dying tree. They are desperately pruning one that will not stop growing.

**Theme:** Abundance as threat. The terror of plenty. Life without restraint. The pruning knife as the most sacred tool.

#### Solheart -- Summer (The Scorched Throne)

A tree burning from the outside in. Crown fires. Drought in the roots. Its people are desert-pragmatists: nothing wasted, nothing sentimental. Liquid Aether here runs hot and unstable -- raw Aether burns rather than illuminates. Amber from Solheart cracks under pressure; its Memory Seeds contain only heat-scorched fragments.

**Theme:** Excess and endurance. The forge-heat of peak power. Glory and its cost. Strength that consumes itself.

#### Ashenmere -- Winter (The Still Place)

A tree that has stopped entirely. No Root-Song. No sap flow. No apparent Blight. Crystalline, ice-encased. Whether Ashenmere is dead, dormant, or waiting is unknown. Its silence is considered a bad omen by all Grove cultures. The only Grove where Hollows stand motionless rather than advancing.

**Theme:** Stillness and forgetting. Preservation vs. stagnation. The beauty of death. What endures when everything else stops.

### The Root-Roads

Ancient sealed passages deep beneath each Grove, connecting all four world-trees. Built and maintained by the Arborists during the Golden Age. The First Druids walked these roads freely to tend the seasonal cycle. Now they are sealed, forgotten, overgrown, warded.

Each passage carries the character of the season it leads to: a road to Verdhollow drips with sap and sprouts vines; a road to Ashenmere grows cold and silent with frost. Navigating Root-Roads requires a Dowsing Rod or a successful Wits check (DC 16 Heroic). The Blight travels Root-Roads. Opening a sealed passage is never without consequence.

**Campaign Hook:** The Root-Roads are the multiverse spine. Each Grove is its own full campaign setting, connected by these deep passages.

### The Heartwood -- The First Tree (Within)

The Heartwood is not a place you travel to by going up, down, or across. It is a place you travel to by going **inward** -- deeper into the wood itself, past growth rings that represent centuries of the tree's life, until you reach the very first ring. The center. The seed from which all four Groves grew.

All four Seasonal Groves are branches of one original tree. The Heartwood is its trunk -- not physically beneath or above the Groves, but **inside** them. Every wall in every dungeon zone is made of wood that, if you bore deep enough through its grain, eventually becomes Heartwood. The oldest wood. The First Growth.

**What It Is:**

- The Arborists' most sacred space. The place where the Root-Song was first sung.
- Architecture that is not carved but grown -- sap channels become hallways, knots become chambers, resin pockets become vaults. You are not exploring a building made of wood. You are exploring the wood itself.
- Each concentric ring you pass through is older. The outermost rings are recognizable -- Arborist construction, familiar materials. The inner rings are alien -- wood so dense it rings like metal, amber embedded in the walls like fossils in stone, light that comes from the grain itself.
- The enemies are not Rot creatures. They are preserved things -- Arborist constructs still executing ancient commands, amber-locked memories that manifest as hostile echoes, and the tree's own immune responses treating you as an infection.

**How to Find It:**

Heartwood entrances are hidden. They are not marked, not mapped, and not obvious.

- Overgrown passages in long-abandoned sections of any dungeon zone -- a wall that gives way, a crack that goes deeper than it should, a root that is the wrong color.
- Hidden chambers behind Amber Vaults. Not every vault contains treasure. Some contain a passage that goes inward instead of a room. The golem does not attack -- it steps aside. It was keeping things in, not keeping people out. The player cracks the seal expecting a Tier IV weapon and instead finds a tunnel of ancient growth rings descending into wood that is darker, denser, warm to the touch.
- Nooks in the branches and Crown zone, nestled out of sight, where the bark has grown over something older.

**How to Discover Them:**

- NPC rumors. Old Cap (Mycelium) was alive when some passages were still open. Twitch's communication crystal may be receiving a signal from the Heartwood. Father Oaken's Chapel pews are carved from Heartwood -- a fragment that could serve as a compass.
- Faction reputation at Allied or Exalted. The Heartwood Elders know where entrances are -- they ARE the tree. The Mycelium network extends into the Heartwood and can guide you to a seam.
- A DC 25+ Wits check in the right room -- the gated check mechanic. You literally cannot perceive the entrance without the gear to look properly.
- Arborist relics: Dowsing Rods at maximum range, Memory Seeds containing the memory of an entrance being used, or tools that let you phase through solid wood.
- Pure luck at legendary gear levels -- a search roll in the right room that nobody expected to find anything in.

**The Vault Connection:**

The Arborists sealed the Heartwood when they sealed the Root-Roads. They used the same technology -- Amber Vaults. The treasure vaults players have been cracking open all game are the same construction as the Heartwood seals. Same amber locks, same golem protocols, same Aether-keyed mechanisms. The player has been learning how to open Heartwood doors without knowing it. The loot vaults are either dual-purpose or decoys -- the real function of the vault system was to seal the Heartwood. The treasure was bait to keep the technology remembered.

**The Moment:** Early game -- player finds a vault, cracks it, gets a Tier III item. "Vaults have good loot." Mid game -- pattern established. Late game -- player finds a vault in a strange location (too deep, too hidden, the amber is a different color, the golem behaves strangely). They crack it. No loot. Just a passage. And the golem bows. That is where the Heartwood campaign begins.

**Theme:** Memory and origin. The answers to every question the setting has raised. Why the Arborists vanished. Why the Rot started. What the Root-Song actually is. The place where the truth lives, sealed behind centuries of overgrowth and deliberate forgetting.

### The Undergrove -- Where the Roots Remember (Below)

The Undergrove is the shared root network beneath all four Seasonal Groves. It is the place where the Root-Roads go when they go deep enough -- below the individual root systems, into a tangle of ancient roots from all four trees, interwoven so densely that you cannot tell which root belongs to which Grove.

**What It Is:**

- The compost layer of the universe. Where dead things decompose and become the nutrients that feed new growth above.
- Not a single tree -- a network. A vast, lightless tangle of roots, mycorrhizal networks, and geological deposits of composted matter going back to the First Growth.
- Time works differently. The Undergrove remembers everything that was ever composted. Walking through it means passing through layers of geological memory -- dead civilizations, extinct species, failed Groves (yes, there were others, before the Four).
- When a character dies and becomes a Hollow, the Hollow eventually migrates downward. The Undergrove is where the Hollows end up -- an entire stratum of the mindless dead, still moving, still wearing their gear, filed away in the roots like nutrients waiting to be recycled.

**The Rot's Home:**

The Rot is a decomposition process. Decomposition belongs in the root layer. The Rot is not invading the Groves from some external source -- it is leaking upward from where it has always been. The Undergrove is where the Rot is native, where it is supposed to be, where it serves its function: breaking down the dead to feed the living. The crisis is not that the Rot exists. The crisis is that something broke the boundary between the Undergrove and the surface -- and the Rot is now doing its job in places that are still alive.

**How to Find It:**

- The deepest vaults in Zone 4 (Rot-Heart). The ones that are hardest to reach and hardest to crack. These do not open inward (Heartwood) -- they open downward (Undergrove). The amber on these seals is discolored -- grey, cracked, visibly stressed. The golems guarding them are damaged, corroded, some broken entirely. The Rot has been pressing against these doors from the other side for centuries.
- Opening one is a choice: you get access to the Undergrove, but you also open a door that was holding the Rot back. This is the consequence the setting has been warning about since the beginning.
- Root-Roads that descend instead of crossing horizontally -- passages that slope downward until the walls stop being bark and start being raw root-mass, tangled and wet and very old.

**Faction Connections:**

- The Hag Circle understands the Undergrove intuitively -- decomposition is their domain. They may be the only faction that does not fear it.
- The Mycelium network extends here. The corrupted nodes that have been causing problems on the surface are rooted in the Undergrove -- the infection runs deeper than anyone thought.
- The Heartwood Elders remember a time before the boundary broke. They know what the Undergrove is supposed to be, and they grieve what it has become.

**Theme:** Death and recycling. The necessary process that everyone fears. The place where the Rot is not a villain but a custodian that has lost its boundaries. Grief, inevitability, and the question of whether the cycle can be restored or whether the damage is permanent.

## Chapter 2: The Rot

### Nature of the Blight

The Rot is not a creature, disease, or army. It is a process -- entropy given biological form.

- Parasitic fungal corruption rising from the deepest root layers of all Groves.
- Cold, grey, and utterly silent. It does not hunger or hate. It simply converts.
- Spreads through moisture, darkness, and stillness. Fire and motion slow it. Nothing stops it permanently.
- Eats living things from the inside out. Victims remain ambulatory and appear functional until collapse.
- The Rot-Heart (deep Zone 4) is where the infection originates in the Burnwillow.

### Hollows

Creatures and people consumed by the Blight become Hollows: mindless husks that retain physical capability and muscle memory. A Hollowed Scavenger uses equipped gear traits with full proficiency (see Chapter 12 for combat implications). Hollows do not speak, sleep, or retreat. They advance. Fallen Seekers become Hollows. They may one day shamble back toward the very village they called home.

### The Root-Song and Rot Silence

The Burnwillow communicates through a subsonic vibration called the Root-Song -- felt, not heard. It is not a sound. It is a pressure in the soles of your feet and the base of your skull. Sensitive individuals (Aether 14+) can interpret fragments: warnings, memories, emotional states.

Where the Rot spreads, the Root-Song falls silent. This silence is the clearest danger indicator in the dungeon.

**Zone-Depth Attenuation:**
- Zones 1-2: Strong Root-Song. Clear impressions available to attuned characters.
- Zone 3: Partial impressions. Fragmented. Unreliable.
- Zone 4: Acoustically dead. The Root-Song does not reach here.
- Emberhome: Alive with Root-Song. The Hearth vibrates with it on quiet nights.
- Amber Vaults: Acoustically sealed. Their silence is clean, not Rot-silence.

### The Blight's Vector

The Rot does not strategize. Its vector is the Burnwillow's central sap-fire core. If the fire is extinguished, the Burnwillow dies. The Root-Roads open. Every Grove falls.

## Chapter 3: Aether, Amber & the Sacred Economy

### Liquid Aether -- The Lifeblood

The golden sap of the Burnwillow. The tree's circulatory fluid and magical substrate. It powers all Arborist technology and flows through vein-channels carved into the trunk walls.

- Can be harvested with appropriate tools (costs 1 Doom Clock advance per extraction -- see Chapter 14).
- Raw Aether is mildly toxic to non-attuned individuals: DC 10 Grit or -1 to all rolls for 1 hour.
- Aether runs are the primary power source for Emberhome's light, heat, and forge-fire.

### Amber -- Vessels of Memory

When Aether hardens around a moment of significance -- a death, a decision, a song -- it becomes Amber. Every shard holds a memory. Attuned characters (Aether 14+) can sense impressions from amber fragments.

Trading amber is not an economic transaction. It is the passing of memory. Spending amber means releasing a piece of the tree's history. Crafting with amber means reshaping memory into form. Breaking amber unnecessarily is a grave social transgression in every Grove culture.

**Sun-Shards** are fragments of the tree's original amber heart -- the most potent amber in existence.

### Amber Quality Grades

| Grade | Formation Time | Memory Strength | Mechanical Value |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Dust | Hours | None | Trace crafting filler |
| Chip | Days | Impressionistic | Minor component |
| Shard | Months | Clear scene | Standard currency unit |
| Core | Years | Full memory | Tier III crafting material |
| Heart | Decades | Living presence | Tier IV component, relic quality |

### Amber Functions

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Currency | Standard unit of trade in Emberhome and all Grove settlements |
| Crafting Material | Required for Tier III+ item construction |
| Sacred Relic | Cultural and spiritual touchstone |
| Power Source | High-density chunks power Arborist constructs and implements |
| Lore Delivery | Specific amber pieces carry readable memories -- the primary lore drop mechanism |

### Sun-Fruits

Sun-Fruits grow exclusively in the Crown (Zone 5) -- the highest, still-burning branches of the Burnwillow. They carry condensed tree-fire: bioluminescent, warm to the touch, redolent of ember and resin. Each fruit is unique; no two have identical potency.

**Mechanical Uses:**

| Use | Effect |
|-----|--------|
| Rot Cure | Halts active Blight infection and reverses early-stage corruption |
| HP Restoration | Heals 2d6+2 HP (bypasses DR -- internal healing) |
| Aether Recharge | Restores 1 charge to a depleted Implement |
| Doom Reduction | Reduces Doom Clock by 1 (see Chapter 14) |

**Why Harvesting is Lethal:** The Crown is the most dangerous zone in the Burnwillow. Apex predators -- Canopy Wardens, Emberstorm Phoenixes -- treat the Crown as territory. Wind currents at Crown altitude impose -1d6 on all ranged attacks. Descent with fruits is the real challenge: fruits degrade if exposed to Rot-tainted air below Zone 3.

### Memory Seeds

Memory Seeds are the core roguelite meta-progression mechanic. They convert individual run discoveries into persistent campaign knowledge.

**Physical Description:** Fossilized nuts or amber-encased seed-pods, roughly fist-sized. They carry compressed Arborist crafting blueprints -- not instructions but living memory-impressions. They glow with soft Aether light that cannot be dimmed or concealed.

**Mechanical Function:**
- Found in Amber Vaults (50% chance per Vault).
- Must be physically carried from discovery point to Emberhome to unlock the recipe.
- Upon delivery, Emberhome's forge masters extract and decode the blueprint.
- Unlocks a crafting recipe permanently within the **current campaign save file**.

**Per-Save Scoping:** Unlocks are scoped to the campaign save, not globally. A solo campaign's unlocked recipes do not carry into a separate multiplayer campaign. Each campaign represents a distinct group of survivors on a distinct run of history.

**The Extraction Penalty:**

| Penalty | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Inventory Slot | Occupies 1 precious backpack slot for the carrier |
| Aether Beacon | Glow cannot be concealed. Stealth impossible. Adjacent-room enemies alerted. |
| Speed Cap | Carrier cannot use the Dash action. Movement limited to standard. |
| Doom Acceleration | Doom Clock advances at 2x rate while Seed is in inventory |
| Tactical Result | Carrier must be protected. Every combat becomes a bodyguard scenario. |

### The Lock-and-Key Economy

Resource management is a primary tension layer, separate from combat.

| Method | Requirement | Effect |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Key | Consumable item | Auto-open 1 locked chest or door. Drops from Hollowed Scavengers, sold at Emberhome. |
| Lockpicking | `[Lockpick]` trait gear | Roll Wits vs. Lock DC. Failure: lock jams or trap triggers, Doom advances by 1. |
| Brute Force | Might check | Opens anything. 50% chance to destroy fragile loot inside. Loud -- Doom advances by 1. |
| Magic | Aether check + 1 Implement charge | Clean, silent open. Consumes 1 Implement charge. |

## Chapter 4: The Arborists -- The First Druids

### Who They Were

The Arborists were the original civilization of the Groves. They were not builders, not smiths, not mages in any conventional sense. They were **Resonance Engineers** -- biological musicians who shaped the living world through sound and Aether vibration. They did not forge metal. They sang wood into shapes harder than steel and wove Aether into silk. This was not technology. It was communion. The Arborist spoke to the tree and the tree answered. To find an Arborist relic is to hear an echo of the old songs.

### Culture: The Symphony of the Sap

- Organized as a guild-monastic order: part scientists, part priests of the tree.
- Their magic was entirely auditory and vibrational. They did not cast spells -- they sang "Notes of Growth" or "Frequencies of Hardening."
- To make Ironbark, an Arborist would sit with a sapling for a year, singing a specific low-frequency hum that signaled the wood to deposit Aether-dense fibers.
- Their cities were not built -- they were "Conducted." They planted a seed and sang the city into existence over decades. A house in the Heartwood was not a structure made of wood -- it was a living extension of the tree's own circulatory system.
- Memory Seeds were their "recordings" -- not written knowledge but first-person acoustic impressions pressed into specialized amber. When a Seeker finds a Memory Seed, they experience an echo of an Arborist's skill.
- Communication Crystals (like Twitch's in the Root-Cellar) were **Resonance Nodes** -- part of a network that vibrated the tree's internal sap-channels to carry voice across Groves. Twitch's crystal still hums because the network still exists. Something in the Heartwood is still transmitting.
- Created the Amber Vault network, Wood-Golem Constructs, Ironbark technology, and the Root-Road infrastructure.
- Their relics are the most mechanically powerful items in the game (Tier IV+).

### The Schism: Silencers and Choir

During the Golden Age, the Arborists observed Ashenmere beginning to go still. Winter was deepening. The natural seasonal cycle -- the heartbeat of the cosmos -- appeared to be slowing. The Arborists panicked. Not from malice, but from love. They could not accept that part of the cycle involved dying.

The order split:

**The Silencers** believed the cycle should not be forced. If Winter was deepening, the correct response was patience -- tend the other Groves, wait for the rhythm to restore itself. Silence was strength. Observation was wisdom. The tree knew what it was doing.

**The Choir** believed the answer was a better song. They proposed singing a resonance that would bypass Winter entirely -- Autumn directly to Spring, skipping death, skipping the Undergrove's decomposition phase. Not stopping the seasons, but editing them. Cutting out the part that hurt.

The Choir won the argument. They sang.

### The Collapse

The Choir's song worked -- partially. They successfully disrupted the Autumn-to-Winter transition. But the Undergrove's decomposition cycle did not stop. It had nowhere to send its output. Dead material kept arriving. Nutrients kept being processed. But with no Winter to receive the recycled material and no Spring to consume it, pressure built in the Undergrove until the boundary between the root layer and the living wood shattered.

The Rot is not a disease. It is not an invasion. It is the Undergrove's decomposition process operating in the wrong place. The Rot has always existed -- it is natural, necessary, and essential. The crisis is that something broke the boundary, and now decomposition is happening on living wood.

Ashenmere did not freeze because of the Rot. Ashenmere froze because **the Choir tested their song there first.** They wanted to "pause" Winter before eliminating it. Instead they stopped it permanently. Ashenmere is their prototype. Their first failure. The Hollows in Ashenmere stand motionless because the Choir's original test-song is still playing there, frozen in a feedback loop.

### The Great Silencing

When the Undergrove boundary broke, the Silencers realized the catastrophe was acoustic. The Root-Song -- the fundamental vibration of the tree -- was now guiding the Rot upward. Every resonant surface amplified it. Every Root-Road carried it. The Arborists themselves, saturated with Aether and resonance from a lifetime of singing, were the loudest beacons of all.

The Silencers made a choice:

- They stopped singing. Entirely. Forever.
- They sealed the Heartwood -- their most sacred space -- using Amber Vaults as acoustic dampeners.
- They sealed the Root-Roads to cut the transmission paths.
- They withdrew into the Heartwood and went into amber stasis, because their very presence on the surface attracted the Rot through resonance.

The knowledge was not lost through negligence. It was suppressed through grief.

- **Generation 1:** The survivors who knew the songs but were forbidden from singing them. They raised their children in silence.
- **Generation 2:** The children who heard only whispered fragments. They remembered music existed but not how it worked.
- **Generation 3:** The scavengers -- Old Cap's generation, roughly 400 years ago. They found the tools but had forgotten the music required to make them work. Emberhome was founded by these people.

### The Choir Did Not Stop

The Choir refused the Silencers' solution. They believed the answer was not silence but a perfected song -- one that could control the Rot instead of hiding from it. While the Silencers withdrew into the Heartwood, the Choir went deeper -- into the Undergrove itself, to sing the Rot into submission.

They failed. Or worse -- they partially succeeded. They did not control the Rot. **The Rot learned their music.**

The Choir became the first intentional Hollows. Not mindless husks -- conducting husks. They still sing, but the song is wrong. It is the Root-Song played in a key that makes living wood rot instead of grow. They are the reason the Rot has pattern and direction. The Rot as a process is natural. But the Rot as an organized invasion -- spreading through Root-Roads, following infrastructure, targeting specific zones -- requires someone giving it direction. The Choir is driving it.

Not consciously. Not with malice, exactly. The Choir believes the four Groves are already dead -- just not decomposed yet. They think they are finishing the cycle the Silencers interrupted. They think they are helping.

**The Choir's Structure:**

- **The Conductor** -- the leader. The Arborist who first proposed singing to the Rot. Now a Hollow of immense power in the deepest Undergrove, the Root-Song pouring from them in a continuous stream of directed decay. The final boss of the entire game.
- **Section Leaders** -- lesser Choir members scattered through the Undergrove and the deepest root systems of each Grove. Each controls the Rot in a specific zone. Kill one and the Rot in that area loses direction -- becomes mindless, which is less dangerous.
- **The Chorus** -- Hollowed creatures and people the Choir's song has recruited over centuries. Not random Hollows. Organized ones. They move in formations. They sing. They build structures out of fungal matter that mimic Arborist architecture -- wrong and beautiful.

**The Choir's Sound (Progression by Depth):**

- **Zone 2:** Occasional dissonant undertone. Easy to dismiss. The Root-Song stutters for a beat, then corrects.
- **Zone 3:** The two songs compete. The Root-Song is thinner; the Choir bleeds through in sustained passages -- a wrong note holding for seconds before the healthy song reasserts.
- **Zone 4 (Rot-Heart):** The Root-Song is absent. The Choir's song is the only music. Constant, low, intimate -- as if someone is singing softly into your ear from inside the walls. Diminished intervals create perpetual unease, waiting for a resolution that never comes.
- **The Undergrove:** The Choir's song is overpowering. Beautiful the way a fever dream is beautiful -- complex, layered, undeniably musical, and completely insane. Prolonged exposure causes sympathetic resonance in the player's gear. The Rot begins to treat them as part of the score.

### Root-Attunement -- A Universal Capacity

Every person in the Groves has Root-Attunement. It is not a bloodline, not a chosen lineage, not a gift some are born with and others lack. It is a universal capacity -- like building muscle or learning language. Everyone has it. In most, it lies dormant. It awakens through prolonged exposure to Aether, through working with living wood, through years spent in the tree's resonant structures.

The Aether score at character creation represents how awake your attunement is -- shaped by environment, exposure, and circumstance. It is not fixed at birth. It can deepen through play.

**The Spectrum of Awakening:**

| Awakening Level | Aether Score | Description |
|----------------|-------------|-------------|
| Dormant | Below 12 (most) | Never activated. Can learn Arborist techniques as rote skills. Tools resonate, not the person. May develop with exposure. |
| Stirring | 12-13 (uncommon) | Awakened by years near Aether sources -- working the forge, farming rich soil, living near vein taps. Wood "cooperates," gut feelings emerge. They attribute it to luck. |
| Resonant | 14-16 (rare) | Fully awake. Hear the Root-Song unprompted. Children raised near Aether who hum unknown melodies. Could learn to sing if taught. Nobody can teach them -- yet. |
| True Arborist | 17+ (the old order) | Lifelong practice + Aether saturation drove physical transformation. Not a birthright -- the result of devotion. Denser bones, extended lifespan. They became what they practiced. |
| Heartwood Descendant | Varies (present) | 400 years of passive immersion. Proof that environment, not birth, drives attunement. Bark-like skin, 150+ year lifespans, extreme vibration sensitivity. |

**NPC Connections:**

- Weld's decades at the Ember-Forge awakened his attunement -- his quirk ("talks to items -- claims they answer back") is real.
- Father Oaken's Aether flame responds to Resonant visitors. He does not know why.
- The Seedlings (Heartwood descendants) want to find surface Resonants who might hear the Painted Songs aloud.
- The "Wake the Sleepers" endgame path requires a Resonant -- someone whose attunement can interact with the amber seals.
- Attunement predates all heritage distinctions. Everyone carries it. Not everyone has had the chance to awaken it.

### The Silent Culture -- Heartwood Descendants

The descendants of the Silencers have lived inside the Heartwood for 400 years. They number perhaps a few hundred. They have built a civilization defined entirely by the absence of the thing their ancestors were best at.

**Painted Songs:** The first generation faced an impossible problem: how do you preserve an acoustic tradition when sound itself is lethal? They painted the songs. Not notation -- relationships between vibrations, resonance patterns, harmonic structures encoded in visual form. Color represents frequency (deep amber for low resonance, pale gold for mid-tones, white for highest harmonics). Brushstroke direction represents phase. Layering represents overtone structure. Every Heartwood child learns to read the murals before they learn to walk. They know exactly what the Root-Song sounds like. They have never made a sound.

**The Gardens:** Where the Arborists shaped wood through song, the descendants shape it through patient physical manipulation. They graft, prune, bend branches over years. It takes a Silent gardener ten years to grow what an Arborist could sing into existence in an afternoon. They do not mind. They have had 400 years. Every surface has been touched. Flowers grow in patterns that, seen from above, form the shapes of the Painted Songs -- gardens that are also musical scores.

**Communication:** Four modes, each with different emotional weight:

| Mode | Weight | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Touch-signs | Daily coordination | Tactile sign language pressed into palms, wrists, forearms. 400 years of evolution has made it as nuanced as any spoken language. |
| The Whisper | Sacred trust | A whisper does not resonate -- turbulent airflow without vocal cord vibration. The Choir cannot detect it. To whisper to someone is to trust them with the most dangerous thing you have. |
| Memory Seed notes | Permanence | Short experiential impressions pressed into tiny amber seeds. Their letters, books, and legal documents. |
| Painted Songs | Eternity | For the knowledge that must outlast the tree. |

**The Whisper-Key** makes acoustic sense in this context. It converts a whisper into the specific resonance frequency needed to open an Amber Vault seal, but only a whisper. It amplifies breath without adding harmonics. The Arborists designed the vault system to be openable only by the safest form of sound.

**What They Fear:** Sound. Any sound. Not metaphorically -- the way someone with severe burns fears fire. They have lost people to carelessness. A child who hummed. A dreaming elder who moaned in their sleep. Each time, within hours, the Rot pressed inward toward the source. When a surface-dweller arrives -- clomping, breathing loudly, talking -- the descendants' reaction is not hostility. It is animal terror. They grab the newcomer, press a hand over their mouth, and drag them to a deep chamber before attempting to communicate.

**First contact:** The player is grabbed, silenced, dragged to a deep chamber. Touch-signs, frantic gestures. Then the eldest descendant leans close. The player feels breath on their ear. A whisper: *"You are so loud."* The first words spoken to a surface-dweller in 400 years.

**Descendant Factions:**

- **The Roots** -- the majority. Conservative. The silence has worked for 400 years. They do not want rescue. They want the door closed again.
- **The Seedlings** -- the young. Curious. They have read the Painted Songs and burn to hear them for real. Most likely to help the player. Most likely to do something reckless.
- **The Grafters** -- pragmatists. The Heartwood is slowly losing ground to the Choir's inward pressure. They will trade Arborist knowledge for military support against Rot incursions.

### The Endgame Choice

The player must choose:

1. **Silence the Choir.** Kill the Conductor, destroy the Section Leaders. The Rot becomes mindless -- still present, still dangerous, but no longer directed. The tree does not heal, but it stops getting worse. The Silencers' solution, extended forever.

2. **Complete the Song.** The Choir was right about one thing -- the cycle needs to complete. Winter cannot be skipped. Help the Choir's song reach completion, but fix it -- add the notes the original Arborists left out. Let the Undergrove finish its work in a controlled way. The tree partially dies and partially regrows. Ashenmere thaws. Burnwillow loses its fire-leaves. The world changes permanently.

3. **Wake the Sleepers.** Revive the amber-sealed Arborists. They know the original song. Maybe they can sing something new -- not the broken cycle, not silence, but an actual solution. The hardest path, the most uncertain, and the one that requires the most faction support.

## Chapter 5: The Six Factions

Every faction in the Burnwillow is grounded in a real ecological relationship that willow trees have with other organisms. The tree is alive -- it has an immune system, a circulatory system, a communication network, and symbiotes that depend on it as much as it depends on them. The Rot has disrupted all of these relationships. The factions are what remains.

Players interact with factions through a reputation system (see Faction Reputation, below). Factions provide services, exclusive gear, crafting stations, and quest lines. Not all factions can be maxed. Some are fundamentally opposed. Choices matter.

### The Hive -- "We Remember What You Forget"

**Ecological Basis:** Bees and wasps are among the earliest pollinators of willow trees. Without them, no new growth. True mutualism.

**Culture:** The Hive operates as a collective intelligence. Individual members have names, personal memories, and distinct personalities -- but they share a deeper communal memory through royal jelly infused with Aether. When a Hive member dies, the collective remembers everything they knew. They do not mourn individuals. They mourn gaps in knowledge.

**Headquarters:** The Apiary -- a massive honeycombed structure grown into the mid-trunk, between Zones 1 and 2. Wax walls glow amber from stored Aether. The air vibrates with subsonic humming.

**Services:**

| Service | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Wax-Sealed Armor | Natural resistance to moisture and Rot infiltration |
| Honey Potions | Healing + temporary stat boost hybrids |
| Propolis Sealant | Field repair: restore 1 DR to damaged armor without a forge |

**Exalted Reward:** Royal Jelly -- consumable that grants temporary hive-sense (see through walls for 3 rooms, sense all enemies on the floor).

### The Mycelium -- "The Roots Beneath the Roots"

**Ecological Basis:** Mycorrhizal fungi form underground networks that connect tree roots, exchanging nutrients and chemical signals. The real-world "wood wide web."

**Culture:** The Mycelium experiences time differently. A "quick" response takes hours. A decision takes days. They speak in spore-bursts -- a tingling on the skin, an impression in the mind. Some members have been alive since the Arborists walked the Groves.

**Headquarters:** The Undernet -- a vast network of fungal corridors that mirrors the Root-Roads. The largest node is in Zone 2, connecting to all four Grove root systems -- though connections to Ashenmere have gone dark.

**Services:**

| Service | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Spore Grenades | Potent but unstable alchemical bomb ingredients |
| Network Fast-Travel | Travel between discovered Mycelium nodes, bypassing rooms and Doom Clock ticks (Friendly rep required) |
| Decomposition Crafting | Break down items into raw materials at better yield. Takes 3 rooms. |

**Exalted Reward:** Mycelium Map -- reveals hidden rooms and secret passages on each floor.

### The Dam-Wrights -- "Build It Right, Build It Once"

**Ecological Basis:** Beavers eat willow bark and use willow branches for construction. Their dams create wetland habitat where willows thrive. Genuine mutualism.

**Culture:** Practical, industrious, no-nonsense. Every problem is an engineering problem. They maintain the Root-Roads' physical infrastructure and take immense pride in their work.

**Headquarters:** The Lodgework -- a massive reinforced structure at the base of Zone 1. Controls the primary sap-flow dam regulating moisture through the lower Zones.

**Services:**

| Service | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Gear Reinforcement | Add +1 DR to any armor piece (max 2 reinforcements per item) |
| Root-Road Maintenance | Open shortcut passages between Zones (Allied rep) |
| Siege Equipment | Portable barricades for room defense. Halve enemies that can attack simultaneously. |

**Exalted Reward:** Lodgecraft -- build temporary fortifications (barricades, chokepoints, rest shelters that halve Doom gain).

### The Canopy Court -- "Beauty Is Not Kindness"

**Ecological Basis:** Moth specialists, warblers, and canopy spiders whose entire life cycles depend on willow trees. The creatures of the upper reaches, where the tree meets the sky.

**Culture:** Beautiful, alien, and cruel in the way nature is cruel. Their bargains are always fair but never kind. Their art is woven from Aether-infused silk. More Midsummer Night's Dream than Tinkerbell.

**Headquarters:** The Crownhall -- a palace of living branches at the apex of Zone 5. Wind currents serve as corridors. Moth-scale dust glitters in the air like permanent fireflies.

**Services:**

| Service | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Silk-Weaving | Enchant gear with Aether-based affixes. Primary source of magical item modification. |
| Wind-Riding | Access the Crown without the lethal climb (Allied rep). Wind currents carry passengers in silk cradles. |
| Moth-Scale Powder | Alchemy ingredient for illusion and stealth effects |

**Exalted Reward:** Mothwing Cloak -- once per floor, become invisible for one room transition (skip all encounters including Doom Clock advancement).

### The Hag Circle -- "Everything Rots. We Just Make It Useful."

**Ecological Basis:** Bracket fungi and decomposing organisms break down dead wood and recycle nutrients. The tree needs death to make room for life.

**Culture:** Three sisters. They trade in secrets, debts, and body parts. They are not evil -- they are the composting process personified. Their help always has a price, and the price is always fair but never comfortable.

**Headquarters:** The Mulching Ground -- a clearing in Zone 2. Three crooked huts around a central cauldron that never stops boiling. Paradoxically, the most fertile place in the Burnwillow.

**Services:**

| Service | Description |

**Exalted Reward:** Blight Tolerance -- passive resistance to Rot damage; Blighted rooms reveal their secrets to you.

### The Heartwood Elders -- "We Were Here Before You Named Us"

**Ecological Basis:** The oldest parts of a willow tree are its heartwood. In the Burnwillow, the heartwood is not dead. It is the oldest living thing in existence. It has just been thinking very, very slowly.

**Culture:** The Heartwood Elders are the tree. They are the oldest branches and root-masses that have accumulated enough Aether over millennia to achieve something like consciousness. They do not move. They communicate by growing -- a new branch pointing at a hidden passage, a root shifting to block a dangerous path, a knot forming in the shape of a face that watches you pass.

**Headquarters:** Everywhere and nowhere. Distributed throughout the tree's structure. The densest concentration is in the deep heartwood between Zones 3 and 4.

**Services:**

| Service | Description |

**Exalted Reward:** Root-Sense -- feel the tree's pain. Doom events give advance warning with direction and approximate threat level.

## Chapter 6: Character Creation

### The Four Stats

Every character in Burnwillow is defined by four attributes called the Four Pillars. There are no classes. There are no levels. Your stats determine your baseline. Your gear determines everything else (see Chapter 7).

| Stat | Abbr. | Governs |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Might** | MIG | Physical power: melee attacks, athletics, breaking things |
| **Wits** | WIT | Mental acuity: ranged attacks, perception, mechanisms, lockpicking |
| **Grit** | GRT | Fortitude: hit points, stamina, resistance to poison and Blight |
| **Aether** | AET | Magical attunement: spellcasting, willpower, Root-Song sensitivity |

### Rolling Stats

Each stat is generated by rolling **4d6 and dropping the lowest die**. This produces a score between 3 and 18. Repeat for all four stats, assigning each result to the stat of your choice.

### Modifier Formula

The modifier for each stat is calculated using the standard floor formula:

> **Modifier = (Score - 10) / 2, rounded down**

| Score | Modifier | | Score | Modifier |
|-------|----------|-|-------|----------|
| 3 | -4 | | 12-13 | +1 |
| 4-5 | -3 | | 14-15 | +2 |
| 6-7 | -2 | | 16-17 | +3 |
| 8-9 | -1 | | 18 | +4 |
| 10-11 | 0 | | | |

### Derived Stats

| Derived Stat | Formula | Notes |
|--------------|---------|-------|
| **Hit Points (HP)** | 10 + Grit Modifier | A Grit of 14 (+2 mod) yields 12 HP. Minimum 6 HP (Grit 3). |
| **Defense** | 10 + Wits Modifier | Base Defense before armor. Armor pieces add defense_bonus (see Chapter 7). |

### Aether Sensitivity Thresholds

| Aether Score | Ability |
|--------------|---------|
| 12+ | Can attune Arborist relics (full tier benefit) |
| 14+ | Senses amber impressions. Interprets Root-Song fragments. |
| 16+ | Receives clear emotional impressions from the tree: grief, urgency, recognition. |

### Heritage: The Four Peoples

The original inhabitants of the First Tree were one people. When the First Tree branched into four Groves, populations became isolated in different seasonal environments. Over millennia -- long before the Golden Age, long before the Arborists organized -- the tree shaped them. Not through magic. Through biology. The same way altitude shapes mountain peoples and latitude shapes skin color. Except the shaping agent is concentrated Aether in four distinct vibrational frequencies.

Each Grove's Aether resonates at a different frequency. Each frequency pushes biological adaptation in a different direction. Root-Attunement -- present in everyone -- awakens and expresses differently depending on which Grove's frequency shaped your ancestors over millennia.

#### Autumn-Born (Burnwillow)

Shaped by decay-frequency Aether. The cycle of harvest, loss, and preservation. Warm-toned skin (amber, copper, brown). Hair that shifts color with age -- dark in youth, ember-red in middle age, ash-white in old age, like fire-leaves through their seasons. Aether expression tuned to **transition and transformation**: the Stirring sense when things are about to change. Resonants feel the Doom Clock viscerally. **Stat bonus: +2 Grit.**

#### Spring-Born (Verdhollow)

Shaped by growth-frequency Aether. Green-tinged undertones to skin and hair. Tall and rangy. Minor cuts heal visibly within hours. Some have faintly bioluminescent eyes. Aether expression tuned to **growth and acceleration**: the Stirring have green thumbs that actually work. Resonants feel sap pressure, sense where new growth will burst. If any modern person can learn the Arborist songs, it is a Spring-Born Resonant. **Stat bonus: +2 Aether.**

#### Summer-Born (Solheart)

Shaped by intensity-frequency Aether. Dark skin, resistant to heat and light. Compact, muscular builds. Eyes tolerant of extreme brightness. Aether expression tuned to **force and intensity**: the Stirring hit harder than their frame suggests through unconscious Aether channeling. Resonants sense systems at peak capacity, know when something is about to break. **Stat bonus: +2 Might.**

#### Winter-Born (Ashenmere)

Shaped by stillness-frequency Aether. Pale, almost translucent skin. White or silver hair from birth. Slight builds, extremely slow metabolism. Natural lifespan 120+ without Heartwood immersion. Cold does not bother them. Aether expression tuned to **perception and stillness**: the Stirring notice things others miss. Resonants perceive the Root-Song even where it is weakest -- the only people who might detect what is wrong with the Choir's song. **Stat bonus: +2 Wits.**

#### Heritage at the Table

Heritage gives one thing: a **+2 bonus to one stat score** at character creation. No complex racial abilities. One stat bump, flavored by Grove of origin.

| People | Grove | Aether Affinity | Stat Bonus |
|--------|-------|----------------|-----------|
| Autumn-Born | Burnwillow | Transition | +2 Grit |
| Spring-Born | Verdhollow | Growth | +2 Aether |
| Summer-Born | Solheart | Intensity | +2 Might |
| Winter-Born | Ashenmere | Stillness | +2 Wits |

Most Emberhome characters are Autumn-Born. Others are available with a sentence of backstory explaining how their family ended up here. Root-Attunement functions identically in all four peoples -- Aether score determines awakening level regardless of heritage. But the expression of attunement is colored by ancestry -- the frequency your ancestors were shaped by influences how you perceive the Root-Song.

**Emberhome's Population:** Primarily Autumn-Born. Spring-Born (Verdhollow refugees) arrived first and form a significant minority -- they brought agricultural knowledge and healing traditions. Summer-Born are rare and valued for physical capabilities and forge-work. Winter-Born are almost nonexistent -- pale, quiet people who arrived decades ago and said almost nothing about where they came from. If true, they know something about what Ashenmere's stillness really is.

## Chapter 7: The GearGrid

### "You Are What You Wear"

There is no experience. There is no leveling. The GearGrid is your character sheet's living edge -- the ten equipment slots that define what you can do, what you can survive, and who you are in the world.

### The Ten Slots

| Slot | Default Stat Pool | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| R.Hand | Might | Primary weapon hand |
| L.Hand | Might | Off-hand weapon or shield |
| Head | Wits | Helms, circlets, masks |
| Arms | Wits | Gloves, bracers, gauntlets |
| Chest | Grit | Armor, robes, jerkins |
| Legs | Grit | Boots, greaves, leg armor |
| Shoulders | *Wildcard* | Cloaks, pauldrons, mantles |
| Neck | *Wildcard* | Amulets, charms, pendants |
| R.Ring | *Wildcard* | Rings |
| L.Ring | *Wildcard* | Rings |

**Fixed Slots** (R.Hand, L.Hand, Head, Arms, Chest, Legs) contribute +1d6 each to the stat pool listed above.

**Wildcard Slots** (Shoulders, Neck, R.Ring, L.Ring) contribute dice to whichever stat pool the item declares. A Cloak of Shadows contributes to Wits. A Spiked Pauldron contributes to Grit. The item decides, not the slot.

### Gear Tiers

Every equipped item contributes exactly **+1d6** to its stat pool, regardless of tier. Tier does not determine dice count -- it determines the **stat score bonus** and trait scaling that make higher-tier gear meaningfully more powerful.

| Tier | Material (Weapon/Armor) | Dice Bonus | Stat Score Bonus | Typical Source |
|------|------------------------|------------|------------------|----------------|
| 0 | Unarmed / Naked | +0d6 | +0 | Starting state |
| I | Scrap / Wood | +1d6 | +1 | Zone 0-1 loot, Emberhome vendors |
| II | Iron / Leather | +1d6 | +2 | Zone 1-2 loot, forge crafting |
| III | Steel / Silver | +1d6 | +3 | Zone 2-3 loot, Amber Vault finds |
| IV | Mithral / Gold | +1d6 | +4 | Zone 3-5 loot, Rot Hunter drops, Vault artifacts |

**Why Tier Matters:** Every equipped item adds +1d6 to the pool (meaning you fill your 5d6 cap by equipping five items in one stat), but higher-tier gear provides a larger flat bonus to the stat score itself. A Tier II longsword adds +1d6 dice AND +2 to your Might score. A Tier IV greatsword adds the same +1d6 but +4 to Might instead. Since the modifier formula is `(score - 10) // 2`, every 2 points of gear bonus equals +1 modifier. Tier raises your floor (the modifier added to every roll), while item count raises your ceiling (the number of dice rolled). At higher tiers, the flat bonuses stack across all ten slots to produce score totals that trivialize low-DC checks entirely (see Passive Threshold, Chapter 11). Trait scaling also follows tier -- a Tier IV [Cleave] hits three targets where a Tier I [Cleave] hits one (see Chapter 10).

### Dice Pool Calculation

Your dice pool for any check is:

> **1d6 (base) + 1d6 per equipped item in that stat pool**

The maximum dice pool is **5d6** (hard cap). Additional gear in a capped pool does not add more dice -- but it still contributes its stat score bonus, set bonuses, affixes, and trait abilities that define your build (see Chapter 17).

**Example:** A character with a Tier II longsword (R.Hand, +1d6 Might), Tier I shield (L.Hand, +1d6 Might), and base die rolls 1 + 1 + 1 = **3d6** for Might checks. The longsword also adds +2 to Might score and the shield adds +1, for a total of +3 to the stat (raising the modifier by +1). Equipping two more Might items hits the 5d6 cap.

### Damage Reduction (DR)

Armor pieces provide DR, which reduces incoming damage. DR is subtracted from raw damage before HP loss. Minimum 1 damage always gets through (see Chapter 12).

### Two-Handed Weapons

Items with the `two_handed` property occupy R.Hand and clear L.Hand. Equipping a one-handed item in either hand slot while a two-handed weapon is equipped removes the two-handed weapon.

### Special Traits

Gear items can carry special trait tags like `[Lockpick]`, `[Intercept]`, `[Cleave]`, or `[Ranged]`. These unlock specific abilities during gameplay (see Chapter 10 for the complete trait reference).

## Chapter 8: Example Builds

There are no classes in Burnwillow. There are no skill trees. Every ability your character has comes from a piece of gear they are wearing. Swap the gear, swap the identity. The following are example builds that illustrate how gear choices create distinct playstyles — not rules to follow, but patterns that emerge naturally from the loot you find.

### The Frontline (Might-Focused)

Stack Might-pool gear in your hands, shoulders, and rings. Equip weapons with [Cleave] or [Shockwave] to hit multiple enemies. Pair with Warden's Watch set pieces (see Chapter 17) for DR bonuses that let you stay in melee. Your dice pool is 5d6 Might with Tier III+ weapons, and every trait you activate hits harder because your gear is built for it.

*Key traits to look for:* [Cleave], [Shockwave], [Whirlwind], [Charge]
*Key gear:* Heartwood Greatsword, Spiked Pauldrons, Iron Band, any weapon with the Keen affix

### The Warden (Grit-Focused)

Fill Chest, Legs, and Head with DR-heavy armor. Equip a shield with [Intercept] in L.Hand and a weapon in R.Hand. Your dice pool is modest, but your DR means enemies bounce off you while your party does the damage. The Rot Hunter's Trophy set (see Chapter 17) turns you into a Blight-killer.

*Key traits to look for:* [Intercept], [Guard], [Aegis]
*Key gear:* Heartwood Tower Shield, Heartwood Armor, Heartwood Greaves, any armor with the Rooted affix

### The Channeler (Aether-Focused)

Equip a staff or wand in R.Hand, robes in Chest, rings and neck items that feed Aether. Wildcard slots are your best friends — Shoulders, Neck, and both Rings can all feed Aether if the items allow it. Your dice pool can reach 5d6 Aether without touching your Might or Wits pools at all. Use [Inferno], [Tempest], or [Voidgrip] to devastate groups.

*Key traits to look for:* [Inferno], [Tempest], [Voidgrip], [Bolster], [Renewal]
*Key gear:* Moonstone Staff, Aetherial Vestments, Signet of Flame, Ring of the Burnwillow, any item with ...of the Canopy affix

### The Ghost (Wits-Focused)

Ranged weapon in R.Hand, Burglar's Gloves in Arms, Cloak of Shadows on Shoulders. Win initiative with high Wits, scout rooms before entering, pick locks instead of fighting, and backstab enemies who never saw you coming. The Shadowweave set (see Chapter 17) makes your first strike devastating.

*Key traits to look for:* [Backstab], [Lockpick], [Ranged], [Flash], [Snare], [Far Sight]

## Chapter 11: Core Mechanics

### The Dice Pool

Every check in Burnwillow uses the same core resolution:

> **Roll [Dice Pool]d6 + Stat Modifier vs. Difficulty Class (DC)**

**Dice Pool** = 1 (base die) + tier bonuses from equipped gear in the relevant stat pool (see Chapter 7).

**Hard Cap: 5d6.** No matter how much gear you stack, you never roll more than five dice. Once capped, additional gear still matters — for its traits, set bonuses, affixes, and DR contributions (see Chapter 17).

### Difficulty Classes

| DC | Name | Description |
|----|------|-------------|
| 5 | Routine | Easy for any equipped character. Breaking crates, climbing a knotted rope. |
| 11 | Standard | The 50/50 mark for a character with Basic (Tier I-II) gear. Picking a simple lock, jumping a chasm. |
| 15 | Hard | Requires Advanced (Tier III) gear to be consistent. Bending iron bars, deciphering runes. |
| 22 | Elite | Only achievable for specialized, high-tier characters. Resisting dragon fear, outrunning a collapsing tunnel. |
| 30+ | Legendary | Requires the 5d6 cap and heavy modifier stacking across multiple slots. Swimming up a waterfall, breaking an Arborist seal. |

**Note:** Each DC step of 3.5 equals exactly one die's worth of average value.

### Gear Stat Bonuses

Every equipped item provides a flat bonus equal to its tier to the stat score it feeds. A Tier I sword adds +1 to your Might score. A Tier III staff adds +3 to your Aether score. Since the modifier formula is `(score - 10) // 2`, every 2 points of gear bonus equals +1 modifier. This stacks with your base stat score from character creation. With 10 equipment slots, a fully geared legendary character can accumulate +10 or more to a stat score from gear alone. Gear provides two forms of scaling: dice (added to the pool) and score bonuses (which translate into modifier increases added to the total).

### Passive Threshold

If your Total Modifier + 1 meets or exceeds the DC, you succeed automatically without rolling. This represents a task where failure is mathematically impossible given your current equipment. A character with a total Might modifier of +12 auto-passes any Routine (DC 5) or Standard (DC 11) check. You still roll for dramatic moments at the GM's discretion, but the outcome is guaranteed.

### Gated Checks

Some challenges have DCs of 25 or higher. These are content gates -- they cannot be attempted without serious gear investment. A locked Arborist Vault at DC 28 is not "very hard." It is impossible until you have the right equipment. This is intentional. Players should see these gates early, recognize they cannot pass them yet, and return when they have the gear. This is the Metroidvania principle applied to dice.

### Assistance

An ally can help on a check by granting +1d6 to the active character's dice pool (subject to the 5d6 cap). The helping character must be adjacent and not engaged in their own action. Only one character can assist per check.

### Scaling Reference (with +0 base modifier, score 10)

| Gear Level | Pool | Score Bonus | New Score | New Mod | Avg Roll |
|-----------|------|------------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Naked | 1d6 | +0 | 10 | +0 | 3.5 |
| Basic | 2d6 | +1 | 11 | +0 | 7.0 |
| Advanced | 3d6 | +2 | 12 | +1 | 11.5 |
| Master | 4d6 | +3 | 13 | +1 | 15.0 |
| Legendary | 5d6 | +10 | 20 | +5 | 22.5 |

### Scaling Reference (with +4 base modifier, score 18)

| Gear Level | Pool | Score Bonus | New Score | New Mod | Avg Roll |
|-----------|------|------------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Naked | 1d6 | +0 | 18 | +4 | 7.5 |
| Basic | 2d6 | +1 | 19 | +4 | 11.0 |
| Advanced | 3d6 | +2 | 20 | +5 | 15.5 |
| Master | 4d6 | +3 | 21 | +5 | 19.0 |
| Legendary | 5d6 | +10 | 28 | +9 | 26.5 |

### Critical Hits and Fumbles

- **Critical:** All dice show 6. The action succeeds spectacularly. Double damage on attacks. Trait abilities trigger their maximum effect.
- **Fumble:** All dice show 1. The action fails catastrophically. Weapons may jam or break. Scrolls backfire. The Doom Clock advances by 1.

### Stat Checks

When a character attempts something uncertain, the GM calls for a stat check:

1. Determine the relevant stat (Might, Wits, Grit, or Aether).
2. Calculate the dice pool: 1 (base) + gear tier bonuses for that stat.
3. Calculate the modifier: stat modifier + flat gear bonuses.
4. Roll the pool, add the modifier, compare to DC.
5. Meet or exceed = success. Fall short = failure.

## Chapter 12: Combat

### Initiative and Ambush

Combat begins with an **ambush check** -- can the party catch the enemy by surprise?

**Ambush Roll:** 1d6 + party leader's Wits modifier vs. enemy Passive Perception DC.
- **Success:** The party acts first. Enemies skip their first turn (ambush round).
- **Failure:** Standard combat. Both sides act normally.

### Attack Resolution

An attack is a stat check against the enemy's Defense (DC):

1. Determine weapon stat (melee weapons use Might, ranged use Wits, implements use Aether).
2. Roll dice pool + stat modifier vs. enemy Defense.
3. On success: roll weapon damage dice, apply enemy DR, deal remaining damage.
4. On critical: double damage.
5. On fumble: miss, and suffer a fumble consequence.

### Defense and Damage Reduction

**Defense** is the DC an attacker must meet to land a hit. For characters: 10 + Wits modifier + armor defense bonuses. For enemies: listed in their stat block as "base_ac" or "DC."

**Damage Reduction (DR)** subtracts from incoming damage after a hit. Characters gain DR from armor. Enemies with DR are noted in their stat blocks. **Minimum 1 damage always gets through** -- DR cannot reduce a hit to zero.

### Aetherial Attacks

Implements (wands, staves, rods) and certain trait abilities hit **Resistance** instead of Defense. Resistance is typically lower than Defense, making magic effective against heavily armored targets but less reliable against Aetherial enemies who have high Resistance.

### Area of Effect (AoE)

Several traits deal AoE damage (see Chapter 10):

| Trait | Targeting | Damage |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Cleave | 1-3 additional enemies (tier-based) | 50% of attack damage |
| Shockwave | 1-3 enemies + stun | (tier)d6 |
| Whirlwind | ALL enemies in room | (tier)d6 at 75% |
| Inferno | 1-3 enemies + Burning | (tier)d6 |

### Assistance in Combat

An ally can forgo their attack action to assist another character's check, granting **+1d6** to the active character's dice pool (subject to the 5d6 hard cap). The assisting character must be adjacent to the attacker and not engaged in their own action (no defending, no trait activation). Only one character can assist per check. Assistance is most valuable in the mid-game, when an extra die pushes a 3d6 pool to 4d6 against a tough enemy's Defense.

### Passive Threshold in Combat

If a character's Total Attack Modifier + 1 meets or exceeds an enemy's Defense, they hit automatically without rolling (see Passive Threshold, Chapter 11). A sufficiently geared character auto-hits weak enemies -- a Tier III Frontline with a +12 Might modifier cannot miss a Rot-Beetle (Defense 10). The GM may still call for a roll on dramatic attacks, but the outcome is guaranteed. This keeps high-tier characters feeling powerful against low-tier threats without bogging down in unnecessary dice rolls.

### Enemy Scaling Rule

Enemies scale with zone depth: **+2 Defense and +1 Damage per Zone** beyond their native tier. A Tier I Rot-Beetle in Zone 2 has +2 Defense and +1 Damage above its base stats.

### Targeting Intelligence

Enemies target wounded characters preferentially. The lower your HP percentage, the more likely you are to be attacked. Minions and summoned creatures can be targeted and serve as Intercept shields.

### Conditions & Debuffs

Status effects that enemies, hazards, environments, and the Leech Cascade can inflict. Conditions persist until cured (by rest, potion, or healer), unless duration is specified. A character can have multiple conditions simultaneously.

| Condition | Effect | Common Sources |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| **Sap-Drained** | Trait abilities cost +1 DC to activate. Aether checks at disadvantage (roll twice, take lower). | Sap Leech colonies, Leech Cascade rooms |
| **Spore-Sick** | Hallucinations -- GM describes one false threat per room. Player cannot distinguish real from false until they interact. | Fungal Mite death burst, Spore Clouds, Gall ruptures |
| **Resonance-Touched** | Gear hums audibly. Enemy spawn chance +1 in rooms you enter. Choir-directed enemies prioritize you. | Prolonged Zone 4 / Undergrove exposure, Choir Section Leader proximity |
| **Blighted** | 1 HP lost per room traversed. Cannot be healed until Blight is cured. Cure: Salicylic Tonic, Father Oaken, Hag Circle Rot Processing. | Rot contact, Blight Corruption hazard, Choir Chorus attacks |
| **Shaken** | -1d6 to all rolls for 3 rounds. Cannot use [Command] or [Rally] traits. | Boss roars (Hollowed Bear), fear effects, first Choir encounter |
| **Entangled** | Cannot move. Lose Move action. DC 15 Might to break free (action). | Heartwood Serpent constrict, Glue Bombs, Vine Strangler, Borer tunnel collapse |
| **Burning** | 1d4 fire damage per round for 2 rounds. Ended early by water, Deepwater, or a round spent smothering. | Ember hazards, Inferno trait, Solheart Root-Road proximity |
| **Frozen** | Cannot take actions for 1 round. After thawing, -1d6 to Grit checks for 2 rounds. | Frostbite Flask, Ashenmere Root-Road proximity, Crown altitude |
| **Weakened** | Might checks at disadvantage. Melee damage halved. Lasts until rest. | Sap Leeches (prolonged exposure), Voidgrip trait, Soul Drain hazard |

| **Poisoned** | -1 to all rolls. 1d4 damage per round for 3 rounds. Cure: Salicylic Tonic or antidote. | Venom Coat, poison hazards, Beetle Ichor exposure |
| **Gall-Marked** | The tree's immune response targets you as a foreign body. Healing effects on you are halved. Rooms you rest in attract Gall Larvae. Lasts until cured by Hag Circle or Mycelium. | Contact with ruptured Gall tissue, failed Gall surgery |

## Chapter 13: Magic

### Implements

Implements -- wands, staves, and rods -- are the primary tools of magical combat. They scale with the Aether stat and hit Resistance rather than Defense, bypassing physical armor entirely.

Implements are reusable. Unlike scrolls, they do not break or burn out. Their power scales with tier, just like any other gear (see Chapter 7).

### Scrolls

Scrolls are single-use magical items of considerable power. Using a scroll requires an Aether check vs. DC 10:

- **Success:** The scroll's effect activates normally.
- **Failure:** The scroll is consumed with no effect.
- **Fumble (all 1s):** The scroll backfires. The GM determines the consequence -- typically the effect targets the caster or an ally instead.

### Aether Attunement

Characters with high Aether scores gain passive benefits beyond combat:

| Aether Score | Passive Benefit |
|--------------|----------------|
| 12+ | Can attune Arborist relics for full tier benefit |
| 14+ | Senses amber impressions. Interprets Root-Song fragments. Detects Hollow presence within 1 room. |
| 16+ | Receives clear emotional impressions from the tree. The Root-Song changes in response to the character's presence. |

### Arborist Relic Attunement

Arborist items require Aether 12+ to attune. An unintuned wielder receives only Tier I benefits regardless of the relic's actual material tier. Attunement is automatic for qualifying characters -- no action or ritual required. You hold the item and the item decides if it will speak to you.

## Chapter 14: The Doom Clock

### What It Is

The Doom Clock is not a timer. It is a pressure system. It tracks the party's cumulative impact on the dungeon's ecosystem. Every action has consequence. Every room explored wakes something deeper. Every failure draws attention.

### Core Mechanics

- **Starts at 0** each run.
- **Advances by 1** per room transition.
- **Advances by 2** while a Memory Seed is in the party's inventory.
- **Additional advances** triggered by: combat noise, failed stealth checks, sprung traps, extended Aether extractions.
- **Reduced by 1** when a Sun-Fruit is consumed (see Chapter 3).

### Threshold Events

The Doom Clock has five thresholds. When the clock reaches a threshold, the corresponding event triggers immediately. These are not suggestions -- they are hard mechanical changes to the dungeon state.

| Threshold | Event | Mechanical Effect |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|
| **5** | "The torches flicker. Distant growls echo through the stone." | Enemy patrol routes shift. Adjacent-room monsters become alert. |
| **10** | "Something stirs at the dungeon entrance. The hunt begins." | **The Rot Hunter spawns at the entry room** and begins tracking the party. |
| **13** | "Patrol routes shift. Every enemy in the zone is actively searching." | All enemy DC checks increase by +2. New hazards appear in previously safe rooms. |
| **17** | "The Blight spreads visibly. HP cannot be restored naturally." | All healing items and natural rest are inactive. Tier escalation: enemies gain +2 to hit, +2 to damage. |
| **22** | "The dungeon seals. No escape." | Root-Road exits lock. Must fight to the zone boss or die trying. The Doom Clock cannot be reduced below 17. All remaining Hollows converge on the party's position. |

### The Rot Hunter

The Rot Hunter is the apex predator. It spawns at Doom 10 at the dungeon entry room and begins tracking the party using BFS pathfinding -- it always moves toward the party's last known position and never loses the trail.

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| HP | 80 |
| Defense | 18 |
| Attack | +10 |
| Damage | 3d8+5 |
| DR | 3 |
| Special | Cannot be defeated with Tier I-II weapons. BFS pursuit. |

**Intended Response:** Run. The Rot Hunter is not designed to be fought in Zone 1 or 2. It is a consequence, not an encounter. Parties with Tier III+ weapons and deep knowledge of its patterns can attempt a kill -- and the reward is extraordinary (artifact-quality item), but the cost of failure is total.

### Vault Breach Alert (Acoustic Consequence)

Amber Vaults are acoustic dampeners. Their sealed interiors absorb resonance -- that is their primary function. The loot inside is secondary. When a vault is opened, the dampener breaks. The area that was silent is now broadcasting a resonance signature for the first time in centuries.

The Rot responds locally. Not the tree, not the Doom Clock -- the creatures nearby, drawn to the noise.

**Mechanic:** For the next **3 rooms** the player enters after opening a vault (the "echo radius"):
- Enemy spawn chance increases to 100% (every room has enemies)
- Enemy count per room increases by +1
- The vault room itself always spawns an immediate wave upon opening

After 3 rooms, the echo dissipates and spawns return to normal.

**Tactical implications:**
- Opening a vault deep in a zone is riskier than near the exit -- you must survive 3 rooms of heavy spawns to get clear
- Players can plan: clear surrounding rooms first, then crack the vault and fight through the echo
- Multiple vaults in the same zone stack -- crack two back-to-back and you face 6 rooms of heavy spawns
- The Doom Clock is unaffected. The tree does not care. The Rot cares -- because for a few rooms, the area is broadcasting resonance that was not there before.

This is the cost/benefit of the vault system. The loot is excellent. The noise is dangerous. Every vault is a decision.

## Chapter 15: Death & Legacy

### Permadeath

Death in the Burnwillow is permanent. When a character's HP reaches 0, they are dead. There is no unconsciousness, no death saves, no bleeding out. The Rot does not wait.

A dead character becomes a Hollow. Their body rises, equipped with whatever gear they carried, and joins the dungeon's population. They retain muscle memory and equipment proficiency. Someday, another Seeker may encounter the Hollow wearing a dead friend's armor.

### The Graveyard

Dead characters are recorded in the campaign's persistent meta-state: name, death floor, timestamp, and epitaph. The graveyard is a memorial that grows with every failed run.

### Legacy Mechanics

Death is permanent, but knowledge endures. The following elements persist across character deaths within a campaign save:

| Persistent Element | Description |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Memory Seed Unlocks | Crafting recipes decoded at Emberhome persist in meta_state |
| Town Upgrades | Blacksmith, Alchemist, Temple, Library levels (0-5 each) |
| Deepest Floor Reached | High water mark for the campaign |
| Total Deaths | Count of fallen heroes |
| Unlocked Blueprints | Item blueprints discovered across all runs |
| Legacy Currency | Meta-resource accumulated across deaths |

### Memory Seeds as Meta-Progression

Memory Seeds (see Chapter 3) are the primary vehicle for permanent progress. Each Seed unlocked at Emberhome's forge adds a new crafting recipe to the campaign's repertoire. Future characters benefit from the knowledge their predecessors died to retrieve.

This is the roguelite loop: die, carry knowledge forward, start again stronger. The character is gone. What they found endures.

### Total Party Kill -- The Burnwillow Falls

If every character in the party dies during the same run, the campaign is over. Not "start new characters" over -- OVER. No new characters. No saved Memory Seeds. No Emberhome upgrades. No meta_state carries forward. Full reset. Start from scratch.

Individual death has consequences: lose that character, their gear becomes salvage, their name goes on the Chapel wall. Total party death has total consequences. The roguelike loop only works if someone survives to carry the knowledge forward. If nobody survives, nobody carries anything.

At the table, the GM should mark TPK clearly: the death screen is not a memorial. It is silence. "THE BURNWILLOW FALLS." No statistics. No legacy summary. The fire-leaves go dark.

This is Design Pillar #4 ("Death means something") taken to its conclusion. The threat of losing everything -- town upgrades, recipes, faction reputation, the graveyard of remembered heroes -- is what makes every decision in the dungeon feel like it matters.

### Rest in Emberhome

Between runs, surviving characters rest in Emberhome. The Doom Clock does not advance in the settlement. HP recovers. The forge is available for crafting with unlocked recipes. Merchants restock. NPCs accumulate memory of your actions and adjust their behavior accordingly.

### Gear Sets

Gear Sets are collections of thematically linked items that provide escalating bonuses when multiple pieces are equipped. Sets are identified by a `set_id` on each item and detected by the GearGrid at equip time.

| Set Name | Theme | Pieces | 2pc Bonus | 3pc Bonus | 4pc Bonus |

| Shadowweave | Stealth/precision gear | Cloak, Gloves, Boots, Mask | +2 Scout bonus | Backstab does double on surprise | First attack each combat always crits |

### Randomized Affixes

Items can roll 0-2 affixes when they drop. Affixes add a prefix and/or suffix to the item name (e.g., "Blazing Ironbark Longsword of the Canopy") and provide mechanical bonuses. Higher-tier items and deeper zones have higher affix chances and access to rarer affixes.

**Prefixes** (modify the attack or effect):

| Prefix | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Blazing | On-hit: +1d4 fire damage |
| Frozen | On-hit: 10% chance to slow (enemy loses next turn) |
| Vampiric | Heal 1 HP per kill with this weapon |
| Keen | Crit range expanded: 5-6 instead of only 6 |
| Volatile | +2 damage, but 1 self-damage on fumble |
| Rooted | +1 DR while stationary (did not move this turn) |

**Suffixes** (modify the item itself):

| Suffix | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| ...of the Canopy | +1d6 to Aether pool checks |
| ...of Haste | +1 movement (extra room transition per turn) |
| ...of Thorns | Attacker takes 1 damage when they hit you |
| ...of Mending | Passive 1 HP regen per room explored |
| ...of the Willow | Blight resistance: halve Rot damage |

### Emergent Combos

Certain prefix-suffix pairs create synergies that are discovered, not listed. Blazing + ...of Thorns = fire reflect (attacker takes fire damage). Vampiric + Keen = more crits, more healing. The system rewards experimentation. The book does not hold your hand.

### Post-Cap Overflow

When a player's dice pool is already at the 5d6 cap for a stat, additional gear in that stat pool does not provide wasted dice. But gear is never wasted — every piece still contributes its traits, set bonuses, affixes, and flat stat bonuses. Below 5d6, you are building raw power. At 5d6, you are building identity through the combination of abilities your gear provides. The cap is not a ceiling. It is the point where gear choices start mattering more than gear quantity.

## Chapter 18: Emberhome

Emberhome is the last permanent settlement in the Burnwillow. Built directly into the petrified, stone-like bark of the world-tree's massive lower roots, it houses approximately two hundred survivors, scavengers, and specialists. Its population divides into two vocations: Seekers (those who delve) and Kindlers (those who tend the village and the Hearth). An active Aether vein runs beneath the settlement, and the sap-fire hearth has burned for ninety years without interruption. The exterior walls are Ironbark planks and resin-sealed bark, sufficient to hold against Tier I-II Hollow incursions. The settlement shakes periodically as the Burnwillow thrashes. Everyone has learned to sleep through it.

### The Hearth

A massive hollowed-out burl warmed by a Sun-Shard at its center. The Root-Song is strongest here -- a vibration felt in the soles of your feet and the base of your skull. Six centuries of shuffling boots have polished the floor to a dark mirror. The air smells of woodsmoke, pine resin, and the faint sweetness of active Aether.

**Services:** Rest, quest assignment, lore consultation.

**NPCs:** Elder Kaelen (see Chapter 22).

**Sensory Signature:** Subsonic hum in the floor. Amber warmth on skin. The scent of resin and old fire.

### The Ember-Forge (Amber-Smithy)

A humid, greenhouse-like crafting chamber built around a natural Aether vent. Living tools hang from ironbark racks -- chisels that vibrate, hammers that ring with harmonics. The forge-fire is not fire at all but a column of golden sap-light that Weld feeds with measured care. Kettrick works the secondary bench near the back, surrounded by the smell of hot copper and growing wood.

**Services:** Craft, upgrade, repair, Memory Seed decoding.

**NPCs:** Weld, Kettrick (see Chapter 22).

**Sensory Signature:** Humid warmth. Golden light. The tap-tap-tap of small hammers and the creak of shaping wood.

### The Root-Cellar

A cramped, low-ceilinged shop carved into a root hollow, crammed floor-to-ceiling with salvage. Twitch has organized it by some system no one else understands -- items are sorted by the emotions they evoke, not their function. A broken compass sits next to a child's shoe, both labeled "longing." The air is thick with dust and the faint hum of something electrical buried in the back pile.

**Services:** Buy, sell, identify.

**NPCs:** Twitch (see Chapter 22).

**Sensory Signature:** Dust motes in amber light. The tick of an unseen mechanism. Dry paper and old metal.

### Market Row

A narrow corridor of bark stalls and hanging oil-lamps where Emberhome's scarce commerce happens. Birsa's stall anchors the far end -- always the last one you reach, always the one with what you need. Prices are never posted. Everything is negotiation, barter, and the careful exchange of amber shards that carry someone's memories.

**Services:** Buy, sell, trade, rumor.

**NPCs:** Birsa, Grimjaw, Patchwork Pete (see Chapter 22).

**Sensory Signature:** Lamp oil and commerce. Low voices haggling. The clink of amber on wood.

### Chapel of the Eternal Flame

A vaulted chamber where a single Aether flame has burned without fuel for as long as anyone remembers. Father Oaken tends it, and tends the wounded, and tends the grieving -- which is nearly everyone. The pews are carved from a single piece of petrified heartwood. The walls bear the carved names of every Seeker who never returned.

**Services:** Heal, bless, cure Blight, funeral rites.

**NPCs:** Father Oaken, Sister Tallow (see Chapter 22).

**Sensory Signature:** Candle wax and antiseptic herbs. Warm stone. The quiet of deliberate silence.

### Warden's Hall

A fortified chamber with ironbark doors and a map table scarred by a hundred knife-point discussions. Warden Voss runs operations from here -- Seeker dispatch, threat assessment, salvage bounties. The walls are lined with weapons taken from fallen Hollows, cleaned and racked by caliber. A tally board near the door tracks active Seekers. Some columns are longer than others.

**Services:** Bounty, mission assignment, intelligence, recruitment.

**NPCs:** Warden Voss, Captain Ashara (see Chapter 22).

**Sensory Signature:** Oiled steel and old sweat. The scratch of charcoal on slate. Tension.

### Refugee Camp

Canvas and bark-cloth shelters crowded against the settlement's outer wall, where newcomers wait for permanent housing that may never come. Cook fires burn low. Children who should be louder are not. The people here arrived with nothing and are slowly learning that Emberhome has little more to give.

**Services:** Recruit, rumor, quest hooks.

**Generic NPCs:** Weary refugees, escaped prisoners, rot-touched villagers (see Chapter 23).

**Sensory Signature:** Cook smoke and unwashed cloth. Quiet conversations. A child coughing.

### Town Upgrade System

Emberhome is not static. As Seekers bring back amber and salvage across multiple runs, four key structures can be upgraded. Town upgrades are part of the meta-state -- they persist through character death (see Chapter 32).

Each building has six tiers (0-5). Tier 0 represents the building's baseline state. Each upgrade requires amber expenditure and unlocks new services or improves existing ones.

| Building | Function | Tier 0 | Tier 5 |
|----------|----------|--------|--------|
| Blacksmith | Crafting, repair, gear upgrades | Basic repairs only | Full Tier IV crafting, affix forging, salvage |
| Alchemist | Potions, coatings, bombs | Salicylic Tonic only | Full elixir and bomb crafting, rot processing |
| Temple | Healing, blessing, Blight cure | Basic heal, funeral rites | Full Blight cure, resurrection attempts, DoomClock reduction |
| Library | Lore, Memory Seed decoding, scouting | Rumor access only | Full seed decoding, dungeon scouting, Root-Song interpretation |

Upgrade costs scale exponentially. Tier 1 requires a handful of amber shards. Tier 5 requires a campaign's worth of careful investment. The choice of which building to prioritize first defines a campaign's strategic identity as surely as any character build.

## Chapter 19: The Wilds

Beyond Emberhome's walls, the Burnwillow's surface stretches in every direction -- petrified forests, volcanic fissures, corrupted hollows, and the ruins of civilizations that the tree has swallowed. These wilderness locations serve as overworld travel destinations, each with its own encounter tier and environmental hazards.

### Ashwood Outskirts (Zone 0, Tier I)

The petrified forest immediately surrounding Emberhome. Trees stand like grey pillars, their bark cracked and their branches bare. Paths are worn through the ash-carpet by Seeker boots, but the undergrowth reclaims them within days. Fire-leaves still cling to the highest branches, casting amber light that does not quite reach the ground.

**Sensory Signature:** Ash underfoot. Distant bird calls that might not be birds. The creak of dead wood.

### Rot Marsh (Zone 1, Tier I)

A low-lying depression where Blight-tainted water has pooled into a grey-green wetland. The fog never lifts. Fungal trees grow in twisted columns, their canopies trailing luminescent spore-threads. Movement is slow, footing treacherous, and the smell -- a sweet, cloying rot -- gets into everything.

**Sensory Signature:** Sweet decay. Sucking mud. Fog that dampens sound and light equally.

### Ironvein Ridge (Zone 2, Tier II)

A rocky escarpment where veins of raw iron and copper run through exposed stone, giving the ridge its name. The mines bored into its face are some of the oldest in the Burnwillow -- Arborist-era shafts, most collapsed or flooded. The wind up here carries the tang of metal and the faint sulfur of deep-earth vents.

**Sensory Signature:** Metal tang on the tongue. Wind through shaft openings. The groan of settling stone.

### Charred Plains (Zone 2, Tier II)

An open expanse of flat, cracked bark-earth where a fire swept through and left nothing standing. The horizon is an unbroken line of grey under a canopy too high to see. Wind scours the surface clean. There is nowhere to hide, and sound carries for miles.

**Sensory Signature:** Wind without obstruction. Cracked earth. The silence of emptied land.

### Windbreak Pass (Zone 2, Tier II)

A narrow mountain defile between two root-ridges, carved by ancient water flow. The wind channels through at cutting speed, and the walls are scored with horizontal grooves from centuries of airborne debris. Scout Lira mapped a safe route through, marked with her blue-painted cairns.

**Sensory Signature:** Screaming wind. Stone dust. The rattle of loose scree.

### Ember Peaks (Zone 3, Tier III)

Volcanic fissures where superheated Aether vents breach the surface. The stone is black glass and the air shimmers with heat distortion. Cinder Wyrms nest in the deeper vents, and the ground itself is unreliable -- what looks solid may be a thin crust over molten slag.

**Sensory Signature:** Sulfur and scorched stone. Radiant heat. The hiss of escaping steam.

### Blightwood Hollow (Zone 3, Tier III)

Deep forest where the Rot has advanced unchecked. Every surface is coated in grey fungal growth. The trees are alive but wrong -- bark split to reveal pulsing organic tissue, branches heavy with shelf-fungi that glow a sickly green. The Root-Song is a whisper here, distorted, like a voice through water.

**Sensory Signature:** Wet organic growth. Muted Root-Song. The rustle of things moving just out of sight.

### Sunken Ruins (Zone 3, Tier III)

The remains of a pre-Emberhome settlement, half-swallowed by the Burnwillow's shifting roots. Arborist-era architecture -- grown walls, shaped-wood pillars, amber-inlaid doorframes -- protrudes from the bark at odd angles. The place is beautiful and profoundly sad.

**Sensory Signature:** Old amber catching light. The groan of shifting roots. A faint, almost-melody in the walls.

## Chapter 20: Dungeon Zones

The Burnwillow is divided into vertical zones from the roots upward. Because the tree thrashes from Rot damage, dungeon geometry is procedurally generated each run (see Chapter 29). The map you drew yesterday is wrong today.

### Zone 0: Emberhome Approaches

**Depth:** Surface. **Enemy Tier:** I (tutorial only).

Adjacent to Emberhome. Safe corridors worn by Seeker boots, punctuated by root-tunnel networks where new delvers cut their teeth. The Ashen Cellar -- a network of shallow root-tunnels beneath the Approaches -- is the canonical starter dungeon. The corridors are low and narrow, the enemies small and manageable, and the loot barely worth the boot leather. But everyone starts here. The walls still bear the training marks left by Warden Voss's first squad.

**Sensory Signature:** Damp earth and shallow roots. Scuttling sounds. Faint warmth from Emberhome above.

### Zone 1: The Tangle

**Depth:** Root Level. **Enemy Tier:** I-II. **Boss:** Hollowed Bear.

The upper root system of the Burnwillow World-Tree. Gnarled roots form organic corridors. Sap flows like golden blood. The Blight -- a fungal corruption -- rises from below, rotting the ancient wood. Damp, cramped, moss and glowing mushrooms. The most explored zone, and the one that kills the most Seekers -- not because it is the most dangerous, but because it is where overconfidence meets reality for the first time.

### Zone 2: The Stone-Grip

**Depth:** Deep Earth. **Enemy Tier:** II-III. **Boss:** The Rust King.

Cold, ancient. Cracked stone. Arborist settlement ruins protrude from the walls at angles that suggest the tree has shifted around them multiple times. The Ironvein Mines bore into the escarpment here -- timber-braced tunnels following iron veins through solid stone. Clockwork Spiders infest the upper shafts, spinning wire-webs across intersections. The Abandoned Forge sleeps in the deeper chambers, its tools still arranged as though the smith just stepped out.

**Named Dungeon Locations:**
- Ironvein Mines (Upper Shafts): Encounter Tier II. Dripping water on iron. The click of mechanical legs.
- Ironvein Mines (Deep Shafts): Encounter Tier III. Seismic vibration. Warm stone. The golden glow of exposed Aether veins.
- The Abandoned Forge: Encounter Tier II. Cold forge-metal. The faint scent of Aether. Claw marks on ancient workbenches.

### Zone 3: The Aether-Flow

**Depth:** Mid-Root. **Enemy Tier:** III-IV. **Boss:** The Blight Mother.

Rivers of raw golden sap. Exposure grants visions or burns minds. The Aether runs hot here, pooling in natural basins that glow with inner fire. The Collapsed Cathedral stands in the deep chambers -- a vast place of worship for the Arborists, its amber-inlaid columns still standing, its shattered altar home to the Ghost of Lady Ashwick (see Chapter 22). The Rotwood Hollow Depths spread nearby -- organic chambers connected by pulsing tunnels of mycelium, where the Blight Matriarch nests.

**Named Dungeon Locations:**
- Collapsed Cathedral: Encounter Tier III. Dripping water on old stone. Echoes that carry too far. The faint glow of amber inlay.
- Rotwood Hollow Depths: Encounter Tier III. Wet organic walls. The pulse of something enormous breathing. Spore-thick air.
- Ember Peaks (interior): Encounter Tier III. Sulfur and scorched stone.

### Zone 4: The Rot-Heart

**Depth:** Core. **Enemy Tier:** IV. **Boss:** The Clockwork Archon.

Grey ash. Root-Song absent. Pulsating fungal webs. The infection originates here. The Rot Spire rises from the Heart -- a vertical shaft of concentrated Blight, its walls grey fungal matter hardened to stone-density, the spiral passages inside grown, not built. A cathedral of corruption ascending toward the Burnwillow's core systems. The Root-Song is dead here.

**Named Dungeon Locations:**
- The Rot Spire: Encounter Tier IV. Absolute silence. Grey surfaces. The absence of warmth.
- Blight Nest: Encounter Tier IV. Warm flesh and growth. The wet sound of assembly. No Root-Song. No silence. Something else.

### Zone 5: The Crown

**Depth:** Canopy. **Enemy Tier:** IV + Crown specials. **Boss:** The Void Herald.

Vertigo, high winds, blinding fire-light. The Crown is the most dangerous zone in the Burnwillow and the only source of Sun-Fruits. Apex predators -- Canopy Wardens, Emberstorm Phoenixes -- treat the Crown as territory. Wind currents turn ranged combat into chaos (all ranged attacks suffer -1d6). Descent with Sun-Fruits is the real challenge: fruits degrade if exposed to Rot-tainted air below Zone 3.

### The Veins

The tree's internal vascular system. Narrow, turbulent chutes pumping sap between zones. The Veins can be hacked into as rapid shortcuts between zones -- costly, requiring HP sacrifice or Keys to open each Vein access point. Using the Veins means trading safety for speed, which is the Burnwillow's fundamental bargain.

#### Return Gates

- "A stone arch hums with residual warmth. Beyond it, the faintest smell of pine sap and hearth-smoke."
- "Rune-marks on the floor pulse in a slow rhythm -- an old extraction circle, still charged."
- "The walls here are dry and clean. A bronze bell hangs from a bracket, green with age but intact."
- "Light filters from above -- not rune-light, not blight-glow. Actual daylight, thin but real."

#### Hidden Portals

- "The air crackles with arcane energy. A shimmering tear in reality hangs at eye level, edges burning with pale fire."
- "Ozone coats your tongue. A portal of swirling violet light hovers above a circle of runes carved into the floor."
- "Visual distortion warps the far wall. Where stone should be, you see flickering images of somewhere else entirely."
- "A low thrum vibrates through your teeth. An archway of crystallized magic stands free of any wall, surface rippling like water."

### Zone 6: The Heartwood — "The Wood Behind the Wood"

Accessed by discovering hidden entrances in Zones 2-4 (behind certain Amber Vaults, overgrown passages, or with DC 25+ Wits checks). The Heartwood is not a direction — it is a depth. You go inward through the growth rings until the wood becomes something older than anything on the surface.

Architecture is tree anatomy: sap channels as hallways, knots as chambers, resin pockets as vaults. Each concentric ring you pass through is older. The outermost rings show recognizable Arborist construction. The inner rings are alien — wood so dense it rings like metal, amber embedded in the walls like fossils, light from the grain itself.

**Enemies are NOT Rot.** They are the tree's immune system and preserved Arborist echoes: Amber Shards, Growth Ring Sentinels, Amber Echoes (phase-shifting), Arborist Automatons, Immune Responses, and Sealed Singers (Arborists trapped in amber, still singing). The boss is an Ancient Arborist — half-Hollowed, the song still pouring from them.

**Special rooms:** Painted Galleries (descendant murals — possible recipe discovery), Silent Gardens (descendant settlements — NPC encounters with the Whisper), Amber Stasis chambers (sealed Arborists — endgame quest locations).

**Unique mechanics:** No Doom Clock advancement (sealed from the main tree's pressure). Resonance exposure continues. Painted Song rooms can teach recipes.

### Zone 7: The Undergrove — "Where the Roots Remember"

Accessed by discovering hidden entrances in the deepest Zone 4 vaults (grey-cracked amber, damaged golems). Opening one is a choice — you gain access but open a door that was holding the Rot back.

A tangled root-mass from all four Groves, woven so dense you cannot tell which root belongs to which tree. Dark, wet, heavy. The Rot is native here — not an invader, a custodian. Failed Grove fossils (petrified tree skeletons older than the Four) serve as landmarks. Root-Road intersections mark where the sealed passage network converges.

**Enemies are Choir-directed:** Chorus Walkers (formation movement, Resonance-Touched aura), Drift Hollows, Section Leaders (zone-wide Rot buff), Ancient Hollows (wearing recognizable Seeker gear). The final boss is The Conductor — 80 HP, persistent 1d4 psychic damage per round, summons Chorus Walkers, Phase 2 at 40 HP: Song of Ending (DC 22 Aether or Resonance-Touched + Shaken).

**Unique mechanics:** Doom Clock advances at +2 per room. Resonance exposure builds at 2x rate. Ingredient drops are doubled (decomposition = rich materials). Hollow Drift rooms may contain gear from previous campaign deaths.

## Chapter 21: Atmosphere System

The Burnwillow uses a layered atmosphere system that enriches room descriptions based on dungeon depth. Each tier maps to a Thermal Tone -- a sensory vocabulary that shifts as the player descends deeper into the tree. The system operates in three layers: a sensory lead-in is prepended to the base room description, then lexicon substitutions shift specific words to match the tone, and finally style markers inject world-level architectural detail.

### Thermal Tones

| Tone | Tier | Identity |
|------|------|----------|
| HOMECOMING | 0 | Return Gate: warmth, safety, relief |
| WARM | 1 | Industrial decay, rust, oil, heat |
| WITHERED | 2 | Cold machinery, silence, stillness |
| COLD | 3 | Arcane chill, rune-light, resonance |
| ROT | 4 | Blight corruption, reality fracture |
| CANOPY_LOW | -- | Trunk interior: sap, bark, amber light, enclosed |
| CANOPY_MID | -- | Branch network: wind, open air, dappled light, vertigo |
| CANOPY_HIGH | -- | Blinding gold, thin air, exposed |
| CANOPY_CROWN | -- | Pure sap-light, sacred/ancient |

### Sensory Leads

Each tone has a pool of atmospheric sentences prepended to room descriptions. The system selects one at random per room.

#### HOMECOMING (Return Gates)

- "The smell of pine sap and the distant sound of village bells replace the frozen silence of the deep."
- "Warmth seeps back into your fingers. The air tastes clean for the first time in hours."
- "A draft carries hearth-smoke and the murmur of voices. Civilization is close."
- "The oppressive weight lifts from your chest. You can breathe again."

#### WARM (Tier 1)

- "Warm sap weeps from the walls, amber and slow."
- "The air is thick with the scent of heated resin and old wood."
- "A furnace draft pushes through the corridor, carrying amber-tinged smoke."
- "Heat pulses through the roots overhead. The Burnwillow breathes here."

#### WITHERED (Tier 2)

- "The warmth has bled from this place."
- "Dust hangs motionless -- nothing stirs."
- "A grey stillness fills the chamber, heavy and flat."
- "Your breath comes out thin. The cold has settled deep."

#### COLD (Tier 3)

- "Cold bites through your gloves."
- "Frost traces the edges of every surface."
- "The air hums with residual Aether -- sharp, electric."
- "Your teeth ache. The temperature dropped the moment you crossed the threshold."

#### ROT (Tier 4)

- "The Blight-smell hits you -- sweetrot and copper."
- "Reality sags here, heavy and rotten."
- "Your skin crawls. The air tastes wrong -- metallic and alive."
- "Something pulses in the walls. The stone is warm and should not be."

#### CANOPY_LOW (Trunk Interior)

- "Warm sap seeps through bark fissures, amber and slow. The trunk groans around you."
- "The air is thick with resin-scent. Heartwood pillars rise into darkness above."
- "Golden light filters through cracks in the bark. The tree breathes here -- you feel it."
- "Sap veins glow faintly in the walls. The wood is warm and alive under your palm."

#### CANOPY_MID (Branch Network)

- "Wind tears through gaps in the branch network. The ground is far below."
- "Dappled light dances across the bark platform. A bird screams somewhere above."
- "The branch sways underfoot. Don't look down. The canopy stretches in every direction."
- "Your stomach lurches. The wind is stronger here, and the footing is narrow."

#### CANOPY_HIGH (Upper Canopy)

- "Blinding gold pours through the leaf ceiling. The air is thin and tastes of ozone."
- "You can see the horizon through gaps in the foliage. The world is very small below."
- "The wind is a constant roar. Leaves the size of shields snap past like thrown blades."
- "Sunlight hits the sap-veins and they ignite with inner fire. Everything glows."

#### CANOPY_CROWN (The Crown Itself)

- "The Crown opens above you -- a cathedral of golden light and ancient wood."
- "Sap-light is everywhere. It pulses slow, rhythmic, like a heartbeat."
- "The air hums with something old and alive. This place was not made for you."
- "Pure amber radiance. The Burnwillow's crown is a living furnace of golden fire."

## Chapter 22: Named NPCs

Every named NPC in the Burnwillow is built on seven load-bearing pillars: Function, Quirk, Secret, History, Relationships, Want vs Need, and Voice. These are not flavor text. They are the machinery that makes each character feel like a person with a past, a present, and something they are afraid to say out loud. Use them.

### Emberhome NPCs

#### Elder Kaelen

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Quest giver. **Location:** The Hearth.

An old man with sun-darkened skin and a burn scar running from wrist to elbow on his left arm. His eyes are the color of cooled amber, and they miss nothing.

**Quirk:** Runs his thumb along an old burn scar on his left forearm when making difficult decisions.

**Secret:** Opened a sealed Root-Road passage twenty years ago out of desperation. Three Seekers died. He told no one.

**History:** Survivor of the first major Rot incursion into Emberhome. Organized the evacuation. Lost his family in it. Now he carries the settlement's memory so others do not have to.

**Relationships:** Trusted by Weld. Unaware of Kettrick's Verdhollow contact. Respected peer of Captain Ashara. Former mentor to Warden Voss.

**Want:** To see the Burnwillow saved. **Need:** To believe the deaths he caused were not wasted.

**Voice:** Precise, unhurried. Never uses contractions when discussing the Blight. "It does not stop. We do."

#### Weld

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Crafter. **Location:** The Ember-Forge.

Broad-shouldered with forge-scarred hands and a leather apron that is more patch than original. His beard is singed short on one side. Always holding something.

**Quirk:** Talks to items while working on them. Not for luck -- he claims they answer back.

**Secret:** Has been using communal Aether from the settlement vein for personal experiments. Emberhome's reserves are 15% lower than official records show.

**History:** Apprenticed under an Arborist-trained smith in a now-destroyed settlement. The only survivor of that community. Carries the last techniques of a dead tradition.

**Relationships:** Respects Elder Kaelen. Competitive rival to Kettrick. Debtor to Birsa -- she brought the tools he needs and never named her price.

**Want:** To be recognized as the equal of Arborist craftsmen. **Need:** To accept that keeping people alive with imperfect tools is the same thing.

**Voice:** Tactile vocabulary. "This joint's wrong." "Feel that? That's the wood arguing with itself."

#### Kettrick

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Crafter. **Location:** The Ember-Forge.

Lean and quick-fingered with close-cropped dark hair and a habit of standing in doorways. His pockets are always full of something.

**Quirk:** Keeps a small piece of Ashenmere bark in his pocket. Claims it helps him think. It has never been warm.

**Secret:** In contact via Root-Road messenger with a Verdhollow settlement that Emberhome believes was destroyed.

**History:** Came to Emberhome as a refugee from a frontier camp. Learned smithing under Weld, then quietly surpassed him in certain techniques. Will not admit it.

**Relationships:** Rival to Weld -- respects the man, resents the authority. Close with several dungeon Seekers who trade him advance scouting intel.

**Want:** Out. To make it to another Grove, any Grove. **Need:** To understand there is no safer place.

**Voice:** Casual, conspiratorial. Treats every conversation like a trade. "I'll tell you if you tell me."

#### Twitch

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Merchant. **Location:** The Root-Cellar.

A wiry man of indeterminate age with darting eyes and fingers that never stop moving. His clothes are layered with pockets, each containing something he has forgotten about.

**Quirk:** Catalogues items by the emotion they give him, not their function. "This one feels like early frost. That one feels like waiting."

**Secret:** Has found a functional Arborist communication crystal in his junk pile. Does not know what it is. It occasionally hums.

**History:** Nobody knows where Twitch came from, including Twitch. He arrived at Emberhome's gate twelve years ago carrying a sack of Pre-Rot salvage and has been accumulating ever since.

**Relationships:** Mutually wary with Birsa -- they compete for the same salvage. Tolerated by Elder Kaelen, who finds him useful but exhausting. Frequent customer and occasional collaborator with Patchwork Pete.

**Want:** To find the one item that will make sense of everything in his collection. **Need:** Connection to people, not objects.

**Voice:** Rapid, associative, prone to trailing off. Finishes sentences with questions. "You know what I mean? Do you?"

#### Birsa

**Pronouns:** She/her. **Function:** Merchant. **Location:** Market Row.

A woman of middle years with road-weathered skin and eyes that calculate value in everything they rest on. She wears layers of traveling clothes and smells of dust and distance.

**Quirk:** Never names a price twice the same way. Always frames value relationally: "Worth more than that lockpick. Less than your left hand."

**Secret:** Has a Hollow-detection sense -- can smell early-stage Blight infection. Has identified one early-stage infected in Emberhome and said nothing.

**History:** A traveling trader who has walked the Emberhome Approaches for longer than most current residents have been alive. She knows paths between settlements that appear on no map.

**Relationships:** Creditor to Weld -- she provided his tools and holds the debt. Rival scavenger to Twitch. Distrusts Grimjaw and watches his dealings.

**Want:** Profit. Security. A route out if things get worse. **Need:** To use her detection gift before the infected person turns.

**Voice:** Oblique. Never answers a direct question directly. "That depends on what you mean by safe."

#### Grimjaw

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Merchant. **Location:** Market Row.

A man of average height with a half-smile permanently fixed on his face and quick, assessing eyes. His clothes are better quality than they should be.

**Quirk:** Smiles with only the left side of his face. The right side does not move -- old nerve damage he will not discuss.

**Secret:** Buys salvage from Hollow-infested zones by paying desperate scavengers below value. Some of those scavengers did not come back. He does not lose sleep over it.

**History:** A traveling merchant who appeared in Emberhome three years ago with a cart of goods too good for the road he claimed to have traveled. Nobody asks where he gets his stock.

**Relationships:** Rival to Birsa -- she watches him, he resents the scrutiny. Useful contact for Warden Voss, who turns a blind eye in exchange for intelligence. Sells Twitch overpriced curiosities.

**Want:** Wealth. Enough amber to buy passage to another Grove. **Need:** To reckon with the cost of his profit margins.

**Voice:** Smooth, oily, too friendly. "My friend, have I got a deal for you."

#### Warden Voss

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Quest giver. **Location:** Warden's Hall.

A heavy-set man with a shaved head and a prosthetic leg of carved ironbark. His face is a topography of old scars, and his voice never rises above conversational volume.

**Quirk:** Taps the pommel of his belt-knife three times before giving an order. An old superstition he will not explain.

**Secret:** Has been sending Seekers on missions with lower survival odds than he reports. The settlement needs the resources more than it needs the truth.

**History:** Former Seeker who lost his leg below the knee to a Rot Slime in the Stone-Grip. He carves his prosthetic from ironbark and replaces it every season. The collection of old legs lines his office wall.

**Relationships:** Mentored by Elder Kaelen. Uneasy partnership with Captain Ashara -- she handles defense, he handles offense. Protective of Scout Lira, who reminds him of his daughter.

**Want:** To keep Emberhome alive by any means necessary. **Need:** To admit that survival without honesty is not survival at all.

**Voice:** Terse, practical. Strips emotion from briefings. "Objective. Route. Threat. Extraction. Questions?"

#### Captain Ashara

**Pronouns:** She/her. **Function:** Military. **Location:** Warden's Hall.

A tall woman with close-cropped silver hair and the rigid posture of someone who sleeps in armor. A faded insignia on her shoulder belongs to a garrison that no longer exists.

**Quirk:** Polishes her sword every evening at the same time. The blade does not need it -- it is a meditation practice from a dead tradition.

**Secret:** Received orders via Root-Road courier to evacuate key personnel to Verdhollow. Has not told anyone. Is still deciding whether to obey.

**History:** Career soldier from a frontier garrison that held against a Tier III Hollow incursion for nine days. She was the last one standing when reinforcements arrived. She does not talk about those nine days.

**Relationships:** Uneasy partnership with Warden Voss -- she handles defense, he handles offense, neither fully trusts the other's priorities. Defers to Elder Kaelen in civilian matters. Suspects Kettrick's Verdhollow contact -- their orders come from the same source.

**Want:** To protect Emberhome. To never lose a garrison again. **Need:** To decide where her loyalty lies -- the settlement or the orders she has been given.

**Voice:** Military precision. Short sentences. "The perimeter holds. For now."

#### Father Oaken

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Healer. **Location:** Chapel of the Eternal Flame.

An elderly man with large, careful hands and a gentle voice that carries despite its softness. His robes are patched with bandage cloth, and he smells of antiseptic herbs.

**Quirk:** Hums fragments of a song no one else knows while tending wounds. The melody changes with the injury.

**Secret:** Knows the Eternal Flame is dying. Has been feeding it amber from his personal supply to keep it burning. He has perhaps two months of reserves left.

**History:** Was a battlefield surgeon before Emberhome existed as a settlement. He built the Chapel with his own hands around the flame, then built a community around the Chapel.

**Relationships:** Old friends with Elder Kaelen -- they disagree about methods but trust each other absolutely. Mentor to Sister Tallow. Disturbed by the Wanderer -- suspects they know more than they reveal.

**Want:** To heal everyone who comes through his door. **Need:** Help. To admit he cannot sustain the flame alone.

**Voice:** Gentle, measured. Uses medical metaphors for everything. "The settlement has a fever. We need to find the infection."

#### Sister Tallow

**Pronouns:** She/her. **Function:** Healer. **Location:** Chapel of the Eternal Flame.

A young woman with steady hands and a quiet demeanor. Her apron pockets are always full of dried herbs, and she smells of camphor and green things.

**Quirk:** Labels her herb pouches with symbols only she can read. The system is consistent but utterly opaque to anyone else.

**Secret:** Is the unnamed person Birsa provides Rot-Salve ingredients to. Their supply chain is the only reason Emberhome has any healing supplies at all. Neither acknowledges the arrangement publicly.

**History:** Trained under Father Oaken since childhood. Took to herbalism naturally and now produces the majority of Emberhome's medicinal supplies. Works quietly and prefers plants to people.

**Relationships:** Mentor relationship with Father Oaken -- she loves him like a father. Silent partnership with Birsa for medicinal ingredients. Shares samples with Elder Maven, though they disagree on methodology.

**Want:** A greenhouse. Enough light and water to grow herbs instead of scavenging them. **Need:** To speak up. Her knowledge of the Blight's botanical vectors is critical and she keeps it to herself.

**Voice:** Quiet, observant. Speaks in short, complete sentences. "The dosage matters. Too much is poison. Too little is hope."

#### Scout Lira

**Pronouns:** She/her. **Function:** Scout. **Location:** Warden's Hall.

A young woman in her early twenties with a lean build and eyes that are always scanning. Her cloak is covered in hand-drawn map fragments, and blue paint stains her fingers.

**Quirk:** Marks safe paths with small cairns of blue-painted stones. Claims the color "reminds the dark you've been there."

**Secret:** Found her team's remains on her last patrol. Took their amber and told Voss she found nothing. She carries their memory shards and talks to them at night.

**History:** Youngest person ever accepted as a Seeker. Volunteered at fourteen after the second Rot incursion killed her parents. Maps the wilds because standing still means remembering.

**Relationships:** Trusts Warden Voss -- he gave her purpose. Respectful distance with Captain Ashara, who thinks she takes too many risks. Friends with Patchwork Pete -- he makes her equipment and she tests it.

**Want:** To map every inch of the Burnwillow so no one gets lost again. **Need:** To grieve. To stop moving long enough to feel what she is carrying.

**Voice:** Quick, practical, deflects emotion with logistics. "We can talk about that later. Which way are we going?"

#### Elder Maven

**Pronouns:** She/her. **Function:** Scholar. **Location:** The Hearth.

A woman in her fifties with grey-streaked hair pulled into a severe bun and ink stains on her hands. She wears a leather satchel that clinks with glass.

**Quirk:** Keeps specimen jars of Blight samples on her person at all times. They are sealed. Probably.

**Secret:** Believes the DoomClock is not a game mechanic but a real phenomenon -- the Burnwillow's vital signs. She is correct, and the implications terrify her.

**History:** Former Arborist academic from a settlement that studied the Rot as a natural phenomenon rather than a threat. The settlement was consumed. Maven was in the field collecting samples.

**Relationships:** Colleagues with Father Oaken -- they disagree about whether the Rot can be cured or only resisted. Collaborator with Patchwork Pete -- she provides theory, he provides engineering. Uneasy respect with Elder Kaelen -- she thinks he prioritizes morale over truth.

**Want:** To understand the Rot. To find the mechanism of infection and reverse it. **Need:** To accept that understanding something and defeating it are not the same.

**Voice:** Academic, precise, excited by data. "Fascinating. Terrible, but fascinating."

#### Patchwork Pete

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Artisan. **Location:** Market Row.

A middle-aged man with wild grey hair and burn marks on every finger. His clothes are held together with copper wire and ingenuity. Goggles pushed up on his forehead.

**Quirk:** Names all his inventions. Introduces them formally. Gets offended if you do not greet them back.

**Secret:** One of his inventions -- a small clockwork device he calls "Fidget" -- has begun moving on its own. It should not be able to do that.

**History:** A self-taught engineer who builds functional tools from scrap and salvage. Half his inventions work brilliantly. The other half explode. He considers both outcomes valuable data.

**Relationships:** Friends with Scout Lira -- she field-tests his gear. Collaborator with Elder Maven -- she provides theory, he provides practice. Mutual respect with Weld -- different approaches to the same problem.

**Want:** To build something that lasts. Something the Arborists would have been proud of. **Need:** To realize his value is not in what he builds but in the joy he brings to a grim place.

**Voice:** Enthusiastic, scattered, prone to tangents. "Oh! That reminds me -- hold this. No, not like that. Actually, yes, exactly like that."

#### The Wanderer

**Pronouns:** They/them. **Function:** Mysterious. **Location:** Appears at DoomClock thresholds.

A figure of uncertain height wrapped in a hooded cloak of woven bark-fiber. Their amber mask catches firelight and seems to change expression. Their hands are the color of dark honey.

**Quirk:** Wears a mask carved from a single piece of amber. The face behind it has never been seen.

**Secret:** Is the last living Arborist. Has survived since the Golden Age through means they will not explain. Their body is more amber than flesh.

**History:** Appears in Emberhome at irregular intervals with cryptic warnings and fragments of Arborist knowledge. No one knows where they go between appearances. The Root-Song changes when they are present.

**Relationships:** Elder Kaelen has spoken with them exactly twice. Both conversations changed his life. Father Oaken suspects they are not entirely human. The Burnwillow itself responds to their presence -- Root-Song harmonics shift when they are near.

**Want:** Unclear. They speak of "the cycle" and "the reckoning" but never explain. **Need:** To pass on the Arborist legacy before their body completes its transformation to amber.

**Voice:** Archaic, rhythmic. Speaks in statements that sound like quotes. "The tree remembers what the people forget. This has always been true."

### Dungeon NPCs

#### Old Miner Grod

A gaunt, filthy man with a headlamp made from an ember shard and iron wire. His fingernails are black with mine-dust, and he flinches at sudden movements.

**Quirk:** Knocks on the wall three times before entering any room. Claims the stone "tells you if it's safe."

**Secret:** Knows the location of a sealed Amber Vault deep in the mines but is too afraid to go there alone. The vault is guarded by an Ironvein Golem that killed his partner.

A translucent figure in formal robes, her form flickering like a candle in wind. Amber light pulses where her heart would be. She is beautiful and terribly sad.

**Secret:** Knows the location of an Arborist relic hidden beneath the Cathedral altar. The relic is a Whisper-Key -- but reaching it requires solving a puzzle she cannot describe clearly through the veil of death.

**Want:** To complete the prayer that was interrupted by her death. **Need:** Release. To accept that the world moved on without her.

#### The Fungal Hermit

**Pronouns:** He/him. **Function:** Dungeon NPC. **Location:** Rot Marsh.

A man-shaped growth of moss and mushroom, barely recognizable as human. His eyes are clear and sharp, but shelf-fungi grow from his shoulders and his skin has a greenish cast.

**Quirk:** Speaks to mushrooms by name. Has favorites. Gets jealous on their behalf.

**Secret:** Has developed a partial immunity to the Blight through years of controlled exposure. His blood might hold the key to a cure -- if anyone could extract it without killing him.

**History:** A former researcher from Elder Maven's settlement who went native in the Rot Marsh. He studies the Blight from inside it, living among the fungi he was sent to destroy. He no longer remembers his original name.

**Relationships:** Former colleague of Elder Maven -- she thinks he is dead. Uneasy coexistence with local rot colonies. Occasional trading partner with Birsa, who knows his location and tells no one.

**Want:** To prove the Blight can be lived with, not just fought. **Need:** To share his findings before the immunity kills him -- the controlled exposure is slowly consuming his organs.

**Voice:** Dreamy, distracted. Frequently addresses mushrooms mid-sentence. "You see, the spores -- no, Mildred, I'm talking -- the spores have a pattern, if you..."

- **Needlewise** -- Youngest sister, tooth-collector. The only one who believes the tree can be saved.

#### The Heartwood Elders

- **Ashknot** -- The one Elder who thinks at human speed, burned by the Rot thirty years ago. Has discovered the Rot follows Root-Road infrastructure.

### Criminal Elements -- The Rot Within Emberhome

Not all threats come from the dungeon. Emberhome is a settlement of 200 desperate survivors clinging to the last warm light in a dying world. Desperation breeds crime. Scarcity breeds exploitation. Not everyone who lives behind the walls is good.

#### The Scrappers -- Emberhome's Black Market

A loose network of scavengers, thieves, and profiteers who operate outside Warden Voss's authority. They are not a formal organization -- more a shared culture of "take what you can, sell what you can't use, don't get caught."

**What they do:**
- **Salvage poaching:** Entering cleared dungeon rooms before the official Seeker teams can catalogue the loot. They take the best items and sell them through back channels at Market Row, bypassing the communal resource distribution that Elder Kaelen tries to maintain.
- **Amber laundering:** Buying amber from desperate refugees at below-value and reselling it to travelers at inflated prices. Amber is sacred currency -- trading it for profit violates deep cultural taboos, but hunger overrides taboo.
- **Gear theft:** Stealing equipment from Seekers who die in the dungeon. The official policy is that a dead Seeker's gear returns to the communal stockpile. The Scrappers get there first.
- **Information brokering:** Selling dungeon intelligence -- room layouts, enemy positions, vault locations -- to the highest bidder. This information should be communal. It is not.

**Named NPC: Grimjaw** -- Functions as the de facto leader, though he would deny it. Operates out of Market Row. Birsa watches him (see her NPC entry above). Warden Voss knows Grimjaw exists but lacks the manpower to shut him down while also running Seeker operations.

**Why they are not villains:** The Scrappers are symptoms of scarcity. Elder Kaelen's communal distribution system sounds fair but does not account for the fact that some people need specific items RIGHT NOW and cannot wait for the committee to allocate them. The Scrappers fill a market gap. They also take risks -- they go into the dungeon without official support, without backup, without the Warden's intel. Many of them die. The ones who survive have earned their take by a different accounting than Kaelen's.

**Why they are dangerous:** They undermine trust. If Seekers stop believing that the communal system will provide for them, they start hoarding. If refugees learn that amber is being laundered, they lose faith in the settlement. The Scrappers do not threaten Emberhome with violence -- they threaten it with cynicism.

#### The Hollowborn -- A Cult of the Inevitable

A small, secret faction of Emberhome residents who believe the Rot will win. Not "might win" -- will. They have looked at the math: the Rot advances, Emberhome's population shrinks, the dungeon gets more dangerous every season. The tree is dying. Fighting it just prolongs suffering.

**What they believe:**
- The Rot is not evil. It is the natural end of the cycle. (They are not entirely wrong -- the Hag Circle holds a similar view.)
- Resisting the Rot is hubris. The Arborists resisted and caused the Collapse.
- The kindest thing is to accelerate the process -- let the tree die quickly rather than slowly. A fast death is mercy.
- When the tree falls, something will grow from the compost. The Undergrove will do its work. A new tree will rise. The Hollowborn want to be the seeds of what comes next.

**What they do:**
- Sabotage Seeker expeditions. Not violently -- a misplaced map, a weakened rope, a supply cache that has been quietly emptied. Deaths that look like bad luck.
- Spread Blight intentionally. Small amounts. A spore sample released near the water supply. A Blighted item left in the communal stockpile. Not enough to cause a plague -- just enough to weaken Emberhome's resistance incrementally.
- Recruit from the hopeless. Refugees who have lost everything. Seekers who have watched too many friends become Hollows. Father Oaken's Chapel is full of people one bad day away from joining.

**Named NPC: The Quiet** -- No one knows their real name. They communicate through anonymous Memory Seed messages left in public places. The Seeds contain visions of the tree dying -- beautiful, peaceful, like autumn leaves falling. They are persuasive in a way that sermons and arguments are not, because they bypass language and speak directly to exhaustion. Birsa's secret (she can smell early-stage Blight) may lead to discovering a Hollowborn agent, if the player follows her quest line.

**Why they are terrifying:** They might be right. The player spends the entire game fighting the Rot, and the Hollowborn's argument is: "And? What have you accomplished? The tree is still dying. You have just made it hurt longer." The player's answer to this question IS the endgame choice.

**Connection to the Choir:** The Hollowborn do not know the Choir exists. They think the Rot is natural and mindless. If they discovered the Choir -- that the Rot is being DIRECTED by corrupted Arborists with an agenda -- it would shatter their theology. The Rot is not natural inevitability. It is someone's choice. This revelation could convert them into allies or drive them to even more desperate action.

#### The Ash Runners -- Dungeon Bandits

Not everyone in the dungeon is there to fight the Rot. The Ash Runners are a gang of former Seekers who went rogue -- they live in the dungeon, in the upper zones, raiding other Seeker parties for their gear and loot. They know the dungeon better than anyone because they never leave it.

**What they are:**
- 5-8 people who decided that the dungeon is safer than Emberhome's politics and scarcity. They have carved out a territory in Zone 1, fortified a cluster of rooms, and sustain themselves on dungeon scavenging.
- They are skilled -- these are experienced Seekers, not amateurs. They know how to fight, how to hide, and how to ambush.
- They do not kill unless forced. They take your best item and let you go. They have a code: "The dungeon kills enough people. We don't need to add to the count."

**Named NPC: Sable** -- Leader of the Ash Runners. Former Seeker squad captain. Left Emberhome after a dispute with Warden Voss about resource allocation -- Voss sent her squad into Zone 3 without adequate supplies, and two of her people died. She considers Voss a bureaucrat who trades lives for reports. She is not wrong, and she is not right.

**Why they matter:** The Ash Runners are a mirror. They made the same choice every Seeker faces -- go into the dungeon -- but drew a different conclusion. They are what happens when the social contract breaks down. They can be enemies (they rob you), allies (they know Zone 1 better than anyone and will trade intel for supplies), or a recruitment pool (convince Sable to return and she brings experienced fighters).

**The wrinkle:** Sable has found a Heartwood entrance. She does not know what it is. She thinks it is a vault that she cannot open. If the player helps her, she will show them where it is. If the player antagonizes her, she will sell the location to someone else.

## Chapter 23: Generic NPC Templates

Not every NPC needs a name and a secret. The following templates provide lightweight characters for procedural encounters, ambient population, and quest hooks. Each template includes a function, a description, a voice hint for improvisation, and a pool of possible rumors the NPC might share.

### Weary Refugee (Ambient)

Hollow-eyed and travel-worn, carrying everything they own in a single bundle. They flinch at loud noises and sleep in shifts.

**Voice:** Exhausted, grateful for small kindnesses. "Is it safe here? They said it was safe."

**Rumors:** Heard screams from the direction of the mines. Saw lights moving in the Rot Marsh. Came from a settlement that fell overnight.

### Emberhome Guard (Ambient)

An Emberhome militia member in patched iron and leather, standing post or walking patrol. Alert but tired. Always tired.

**Voice:** Professional, clipped. "Move along. Stay inside the walls after dark."

**Rumors:** The Warden's worried about the southern approach. Something's been testing the perimeter at night. Captain Ashara doubled the watch rotation.

### Traveling Merchant (Merchant)

A road-worn trader with a pack mule and a suspicious eye. Carries Tier I-II goods and charges above market rate for the privilege of convenience.

**Voice:** Transactional, wary. "I don't haggle. The road's too long for that."

**Rumors:** The road from the Ridge is blocked by a new Rot incursion. Heard there's amber in the Sunken Ruins. Grimjaw's prices are half mine but ask where he gets his stock.

### Scavenger (Ambient)

A solo operator in mismatched gear, heading into or returning from the tunnels. They keep their finds close and their route closer.

**Voice:** Tight-lipped, territorial. "That area's mine. Find your own."

**Rumors:** Found signs of Arborist construction in the lower Tangle. The Rot's spreading faster in the eastern passages. Someone's been leaving blue cairns on a new path.

### Escaped Prisoner (Quest Hook)

Bruised, shaking, bearing marks of restraint on their wrists. They escaped from a Hollow work-crew -- and yes, Hollows do use prisoners. As bait.

**Voice:** Frantic, disjointed. "They're organized. They remember. They remember everything."

**Rumors:** The Hollows are gathering in the Stone-Grip. There are prisoners still alive in the deeper tunnels. Something is directing the Hollows -- something that thinks.

### Rot-Touched Villager (Ambient)

A resident of Emberhome showing early-stage Blight symptoms -- grey veins under the skin, a persistent cough, a faint sweet smell. They hide it. Everyone hides it.

**Voice:** Defensive, frightened. "I'm fine. It's just a cold. Leave me alone."

**Rumors:** Sister Tallow has a treatment but not a cure. There are more infected than anyone admits. Birsa looks at people strangely -- like she can see something the rest of us cannot.

### Mercenary (Recruit)

A professional fighter looking for paid work. Better equipped than most Seekers, with the cynical efficiency of someone who has been doing this too long.

**Voice:** Business-like. "My rate's five amber shards per delve. Non-negotiable. I don't die for free."

**Rumors:** The Rot Hunter's been spotted in Zone 1 -- earlier than usual. Someone's offering double rate for a Rot Spire run. The last party to hit the Deep Shafts came back one short.

### Pilgrim (Ambient)

A traveler making the dangerous journey to the Chapel of the Eternal Flame. They carry amber offerings and speak in the reverent tones of someone who has walked a very long way on faith alone.

**Voice:** Reverent, hopeful. "The flame still burns. That means something. That has to mean something."

**Rumors:** The Chapel's flame has been flickering. Father Oaken looks more tired each season. Pilgrims from the eastern settlements have stopped coming.

### Apprentice Forge Worker (Ambient)

A young person learning the trade under Weld or Kettrick. Eager, clumsy, covered in minor burns and bandaged fingers.

**Voice:** Enthusiastic, nervous. "Master Weld says I'm making progress. He didn't yell today. That's progress."

**Rumors:** Weld's been working on something secret at night. Kettrick gets messages from outside Emberhome. The forge has been using more Aether than usual.

### Wilderness Guide (Recruit)

A weathered survivalist who knows the paths between Emberhome and the surrounding zones. They charge for their knowledge and earn every shard.

**Voice:** Laconic, confident. "Follow me, step where I step, and you'll live. Probably."

**Rumors:** New paths have opened since the last tremor. The Charred Plains aren't as empty as they look. Something large has been moving through the Ashwood at night.

## Chapter 25: Boss Encounters

Bosses are unique creatures that anchor each zone. They do not appear in the standard enemy tables. Each boss encounter is a set piece -- a room designed around the creature's abilities, demanding specific tactical responses. The five canonical boss templates are presented here with full stat blocks and tactical notes.

### Hollowed Bear (Zone 1 Boss)

**HP:** 25. **Defense:** 14. **Damage:** 1d6+4.

A massive bear, fur matted with rot, eyes burning with unnatural hunger. Its roar echoes with the voices of all it has consumed.

**Special Abilities:**
- **Terrifying Roar:** At the start of combat, all characters must pass a Grit DC 12 check or suffer -1d6 to all rolls for 3 turns.
- **Thrash:** On a critical hit (roll 6 on final die), hits ALL adjacent targets for full damage.

**Tactical Notes:** The Hollowed Bear is the first real test. Its Terrifying Roar punishes parties that neglected Grit during character creation (see Chapter 6), and its Thrash ability makes clustering around the boss a death sentence. Spread out. Focus fire. Bring a Sentinel to absorb the Roar penalty while the rest of the party gets clean shots in.

### The Rust King (Zone 2 Boss)

**HP:** 50. **Defense:** 16. **Damage:** 5d6.

Once a great artificer, now a corroded tyrant bound to his throne of petrified wood. His crown is fused iron and dead amber, and his hands still shape metal -- twisting it, ruining it.

**Special Abilities:**
- **Corroding Touch:** On hit, target's armor loses 1 DR permanently.
- **Summon Rot-Beetles:** Action. Summons 1d6 Rot-Beetles.
- **Ironbark Throne:** Immune to Might-based attacks. Weak to Aether.

**Tactical Notes:** The Rust King is an attrition fight. His Corroding Touch strips armor, and his Rot-Beetle summons clog the action economy. The key is his Ironbark Throne immunity: Might-heavy Vanguard builds bounce off him. You need an Arcanist or a party with Aether-scaling weapons (see Chapter 16). Bring fire. Kill the beetles fast. Do not let the fight drag.

### The Blight Mother (Zone 3 Boss)

**HP:** 60. **Defense:** 15. **Damage:** 4d6.

The source of the Blight. A grotesque fusion of tree and flesh, rooted to the floor by a web of mycelium tendrils. Smaller creatures emerge from splits in her flesh like terrible births. The golden Aether rivers around her run grey and sluggish.

**Special Abilities:**
- **Spore Burst:** AoE 20ft. DC 16 Grit or poisoned (1d6/turn for 5 turns).
- **Root Network:** Heals 2d6 HP per turn while standing in dungeon.
- **Fungal Rebirth:** On death, explodes (3d6 to all in 15ft) and spawns 2 Shambling Moss.

**Tactical Notes:** The Blight Mother regenerates. She will outheal your damage if the fight goes long. Her Root Network healing requires her to be rooted in the dungeon floor -- if a trait or ability can move her (Gust, forced movement), she loses the regeneration. Her Fungal Rebirth means the fight is not over when she drops to 0 HP. Save resources for the death explosion and the Shambling Moss adds.

### The Clockwork Archon (Zone 4 Boss)

**HP:** 45. **Defense:** 18. **Damage:** 5d6.

An ancient war machine, still following its last command: "Kill all intruders." Corroded gears and fungal tendrils. The amber heart in its chest beats with a rhythm that is almost, but not quite, the Root-Song.

**Special Abilities:**
- **Precision Strike:** Critical hits on 4-6 instead of 6.
- **Repair Protocol:** Action. Restore 3d6 HP (3 charges).
- **Overload:** When reduced below 15 HP, explodes (5d6 lightning, 20ft radius).

**Tactical Notes:** The Archon hits harder per swing than any other boss thanks to its expanded crit range. Its Repair Protocol gives it effective HP far beyond its 45 base -- treat it as a 72 HP fight. The Overload explosion at 15 HP is the kill threshold: you need to burst it from above 15 to dead in a single round, or face 5d6 lightning to the entire party. Coordinate. Save your big hits. Intercept shields are critical here.

### The Void Herald (Zone 5 Boss)

**HP:** 40. **Defense:** 17. **Damage:** 4d6.

A creature from between dimensions. It should not exist. Its form is a compression of fire and screaming wind, and its voice is the crack of ancient wood breaking under impossible strain.

**Special Abilities:**
- **Aether Drain:** On hit, target loses 1 Aether permanently.
- **Phase Shift:** Bonus action. Become ethereal (immune to physical damage) for 1 turn.
- **Void Gaze:** DC 18 Aether or paralyzed for 1d3 turns.

**Tactical Notes:** The Void Herald is the anti-caster boss. Its Aether Drain permanently reduces your magical capability, and its Phase Shift forces you to alternate between physical and magical damage each round. Void Gaze is devastating -- DC 18 Aether is Legendary difficulty (see Appendix A). Characters with low Aether scores should avert their gaze (forfeit their attack to auto-succeed on the save). This fight rewards hybrid builds that can switch between damage types.

## Chapter 25b: Natural Threats

The Choir is the endgame antagonist, but the Burnwillow was never safe. These threats existed before the Collapse. The Arborists managed them. Nobody manages them now.

### The Leech Cascade -- An Ecosystem Death Spiral

Sap Leeches are massive colonial organisms -- pale, translucent, dog-sized -- that cluster on sap-rich surfaces in groups of dozens. They do not attack unless disturbed. They drain.

The cascade is a chain reaction:

1. **Leeches colonize healthy sap veins.** They target the richest wood -- near Aether channels, where the Root-Song is strongest. They do not seek weakness. They seek the best.
2. **The drained area weakens.** Root-Song thins. Aether drops. The wood is diminished -- not dead, not rotted, just tired.
3. **The Rot probes the weakened area.** The Rot is drawn to weakness the way scavengers are drawn to limping prey. Healthy wood repels it. Drained wood does not.
4. **The tree floods the area with healing sap.** Immune response -- rapid growth, sealed wounds, thickened bark. It works. The Rot is pushed back.
5. **Gall parasites hijack the immune response.** The healing flood is the richest sap the Gall has ever tasted. It redirects the growth into Gall chambers -- tumor-like structures that trap the healing energy. The wound does not close.
6. **Leeches expand.** The Gall chambers leak sap. The area now produces more accessible sap than before. More Leeches arrive.
7. **The spiral accelerates.** More drain, more weakness, more Rot, more immune response, more Gall, more sap, more Leeches. Left unchecked, an entire zone is consumed in months.
8. **The Choir notices.** A Section Leader encountering a cascade can amplify it (direct more Rot) or simply let it run. The natural process does the Choir's work for free.

**The Hive farms Leeches.** Honeydew from Sap Leeches is the base ingredient in Hive honey potions. The Hive has farmed small colonies since before the Collapse. The Arborists kept them pruned. Nobody prunes now. The Hive's "managed" colonies are contributing to the cascade -- slowly, but contributing. Some have gone feral. The Hive is too biologically dependent to admit this.

**Cascade Room Table (Zone 2+, 1 in 6 rooms):**

| Stage | Condition Inflicted | Enemies | Environmental |
|-------|-------------------|---------|--------------|
| 1: Leech Colony | Sap-Drained while in room | 1d3 passive Sap Leeches | Aether vein visibly pulsing, Leeches attached to walls |
| 2: Leech + Gall | Sap-Drained + Gall-Marked risk if Gall ruptured | 1d3 Leeches + 1 Gall Larva | Tumor-like growths on walls, sap weeping from cracks |
| 3: Full Cascade | Sap-Drained + Spore-Sick from Gall spores | 1d3 Leeches + 1 Gall Larva + 1d3 Rot creatures | Rot tendrils on floor, Gall tumors everywhere, air thick with spores |
| 4: Collapse | Sap-Drained + hazard check (DC 15 Grit or Entangled in collapsing structure) | As Stage 3 + Borer nest | Floor giving way, walls crumbling, full infestation |

Clearing all threats restores the room. Partial clearing (killing Leeches but leaving Galls) means the cascade rebuilds within 1d6 rooms of absence.

**Player quest tension:** The Hive asks you to protect a Leech farm. The Dam-Wrights ask you to destroy the same colony. Both are right. The Hag Circle offers a third option: synthetic honeydew from dead Leeches + Gall tissue -- but you need to let the cascade run long enough to harvest the Galls first.

### The Gall -- Parasites Who Build Cages

Gall organisms hijack the tree's healing response to build nursery chambers. They are not Rot. They are parasites that exploit the tree's immune system -- the healthiest wood is the most infected because that is where the strongest healing response occurs.

**Physical description:** Tumor-like bulges in walls and ceilings, 1-3 feet across, surface smooth and faintly pulsing. Warm to the touch. They look like the wood is pregnant. Cutting one open releases a Gall Larva -- a pale, segmented grub the size of a cat, aggressive and fast -- plus a flood of nutrient-rich sap (Aether ingredient).

**Behavior:** Galls do not move. They do not attack. They grow. A room with Gall growths is a room where the tree's immune system is being consumed from the inside. The healing that should be fighting the Rot is instead building Gall chambers.

**Gall-Marked condition:** Players who make skin contact with ruptured Gall tissue risk the tree's immune system treating THEM as a foreign body -- healing effects halved, rest attracts Gall Larvae. The Hag Circle and Mycelium can cure this (they understand decomposition and immune systems respectively).

**Heartwood connection:** The Heartwood descendants use Gall growths as a controlled food source. The sap around a Gall is extremely nutrient-rich. They have spent centuries learning to maintain small, managed Galls without letting them cascade -- one of their few successful adaptations of the Arborists' ecosystem management skills.

### Bark Borers -- The Tunnelers

Beetle-like creatures that bore networks of tunnels through the bark and outer wood. The tunnels themselves are the threat -- they weaken structural integrity, cause collapses, and create pathways for the Rot to spread faster than it could through solid wood.

**Physical description:** Armored, iridescent beetles the length of a forearm. They work in colonies of 10-30, carving galleries in geometric patterns. The galleries are eerily beautiful -- spiraling tunnels with smooth walls polished by generations of passage. The sound of active Borers is a constant, quiet chewing that you feel more than hear.

**Behavior:** Borers are not aggressive toward people. They do not bite. They flee if cornered. But their tunnels are devastating -- every gallery is a structural weakness and a Rot highway. A Borer-infested room has:
- Hazard check (DC 15 Grit) on entry -- floor may collapse into a gallery below
- Walls that crumble if struck (accidentally smashing through into adjacent rooms or galleries)
- Rot spreading faster (the tunnels bypass the tree's sealed bark layer)

**Faction connection:** The Dam-Wrights are the natural enemy of Borers. Structural maintenance is their identity. A Dam-Wright quest line involves systematically clearing Borer galleries in a critical zone before the main dam repair can proceed. The Borers keep coming back because nobody is managing the population -- the Arborists used specific resonance frequencies to repel them, and that knowledge was lost in the Great Silencing.

**Borer Galleries as dungeon features:** Cleared Borer tunnels can serve as shortcuts -- narrow, fragile passages that connect rooms in ways the main dungeon layout does not. Using one is a gamble: faster, but the tunnel might collapse behind you (Doom +1) or you might run into an active colony.

### Crown Predators -- Apex Wildlife

The Crown (Zone 5) is the healthiest part of the Burnwillow -- the canopy where fire-leaves burn brightest, Sun-Fruits grow, and the Root-Song is clean. The Rot has not reached here. The reason is not the tree's defenses. It is the things that live here.

Crown Predators are not corrupted, not evil, not aligned with any faction. They are apex wildlife in their natural habitat. Storm Raptors with 20-foot wingspans that hunt by thermal updraft. Canopy Stalkers -- ambush predators that mimic the pattern of fire-leaves until prey passes beneath. Emberstorm Phoenixes that roost in the highest branches and immolate anything that threatens their nests.

**Why they matter:** The Crown Predators are inadvertent guardians. They eat anything that climbs -- including Rot creatures. The Rot does not reach the Crown because anything Blighted that enters gets killed and eaten before it can spread. The predators do not know they are performing this function. They are just hungry.

**The Canopy Court** coexists with Crown Predators the way herders coexist with wolves -- carefully, respectfully, with ritual offerings and well-mapped flight paths. A player entering the Crown without Court guidance is prey. With Court guidance (Allied+ reputation), the predators are a manageable hazard rather than a guaranteed death.

**Conditions inflicted:** Crown Predators do not inflict disease or corruption. They inflict Entangled (Canopy Stalker web), Shaken (Storm Raptor dive-screech), and Burning (Phoenix immolation). Clean, honest violence. The Crown is dangerous but uncorrupted -- a reminder of what the rest of the tree used to be.

### The Hollow Tide -- Choir Recruitment

The Choir does not individually convert each Hollow. It broadcasts. The Choir's song is a standing wave in the Undergrove -- a permanent resonance that any Hollow within range eventually synchronizes with. This is not mind control. It is **entrainment** -- the same phenomenon that makes pendulum clocks on the same wall synchronize their swings.

**The recruitment process:**
1. A character dies. They become a Hollow -- mindless, wandering.
2. The Hollow migrates downward over time. All Hollows drift toward the Undergrove -- gravity, instinct, the faint pull of the Choir's song.
3. In the Undergrove, the Hollow is immersed in the standing wave. Over days or weeks, its movements synchronize. It starts walking in rhythm. Then humming -- a single note, barely audible.
4. Eventually it joins the Chorus. Formation movement. Building. Singing. Not aware. Not suffering. Resonating.

**The horror for the player:** Chorus members in the Undergrove wear the gear of fallen Seekers from Emberhome. Gear the player might recognize. If permadeath is tracked across campaigns, the player may encounter their own previous character -- Hollowed, singing, building fungal architecture with hands that once held the player's sword.

**Resonance-Touched condition:** The Choir's broadcast affects living creatures in deep zones. Prolonged time in Zone 4 or the Undergrove causes sympathetic resonance -- gear starts humming, the Rot begins treating you as part of the score. The condition makes you a beacon: enemy spawn increases in rooms you enter, and Choir-directed enemies prioritize you as a target.

## Chapter 26: The Economy

The Burnwillow's economy runs on three currencies: amber, scrap, and memory. None of them are simple.

### Amber

Amber is the standard unit of trade in Emberhome and all Grove settlements. It is not coin. It is solidified Aether -- the tree's lifeblood, hardened around moments of significance. Every shard holds a memory. Trading amber is not an economic transaction. It is the passing of memory. Spending amber means releasing a piece of the tree's history.

#### Amber Quality Grades

| Grade | Formation Time | Memory Strength | Mechanical Value |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Dust | Hours | None | Trace crafting filler |
| Chip | Days | Impressionistic | Minor component |
| Shard | Months | Clear scene | Standard currency unit |
| Core | Years | Full memory | Tier III crafting material |
| Heart | Decades | Living presence | Tier IV component, relic quality |

### Memory Seeds

Memory Seeds are the core roguelite meta-progression mechanic. Fossilized nuts or amber-encased seed-pods, roughly fist-sized, carrying compressed Arborist crafting blueprints. They glow with soft Aether light that cannot be dimmed or concealed. Found in Amber Vaults (50% chance per Vault), they must be physically carried to Emberhome to unlock their recipe.

Memory Seed unlocks are scoped to the campaign save file, not globally (see Chapter 32). A solo campaign's unlocked recipes do not carry into a separate 4-player campaign. Each campaign represents a distinct group of survivors on a distinct run of history.

**Extraction Penalties:**

| Penalty | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Inventory Slot | Occupies 1 precious backpack slot |
| Aether Beacon | Glow cannot be concealed. Stealth impossible. Adjacent-room enemies alerted |
| Speed Cap | Carrier cannot Dash. Movement limited to standard 30ft |
| Doom Acceleration | Doom Clock advances at 2x rate while Seed is in inventory |

The result: every Memory Seed extraction becomes a bodyguard scenario. The carrier must be protected. Every combat becomes an escort mission.

### Scrap

Bent nails, broken hinges, lengths of chain. Worthless alone, but Weld can make something of it. Scrap is the fundamental crafting material -- what you bring home when you cannot find amber. It feeds the Blacksmith's upgrade bench and keeps Emberhome's infrastructure from collapsing.

### Keys

Consumable items that auto-open one locked chest or door. Drop from Hollowed Scavengers and are sold at Emberhome. The alternative methods of opening locks -- Lockpick trait (Wits check), brute force (Might check, 50% loot destruction, +1 Doom), or magic (Aether, consumes 1 Implement charge) -- each carry tradeoffs that make Keys valuable despite their single-use nature.

## Chapter 29: Procedural Generation

The Burnwillow's dungeons are procedurally generated each run. The tree thrashes from Rot damage, crushing old tunnels and tearing open new ones. Geography is genuinely unstable. The following tables drive the generation system.

### Room Names

Dungeon rooms are named by combining a random prefix with a random suffix:

**Prefixes:** Ash, Ember, Root, Iron, Rot, Blight, Cinder, Hollow, Fungal, Stone, Amber, Dark, Charred, Deep, Broken, Lost, Sealed, Forgotten, Sunken, Twisted.

**Suffixes:** Chamber, Hall, Gallery, Passage, Cellar, Vault, Den, Hollow, Nest, Alcove, Crossing, Junction, Well, Pit, Stairway, Bridge, Forge, Chapel, Barracks, Study.

The combination "Forgotten Forge" tells the player something different than "Rot Nest." The name is the first piece of information a Seeker receives about a room, and it should set expectations -- correctly or deceptively.

### Dungeon Dressing

Every room receives one piece of dressing from the following table. Dressing is flavor text with no mechanical effect, but it provides the sensory texture that makes each room feel distinct.

1. Charred beams lean against crumbling walls, their surfaces etched with the growth-rings of centuries.
2. A carpet of grey ash covers the floor, disturbed by recent footprints that are not yours.
3. Bioluminescent fungi glow sickly green along the ceiling, casting shadows that move when you don't.
4. Rusted chains hang from iron hooks set in the stone, the links fused together by corrosion.
5. A faint smell of sulfur and decay permeates the air, strongest near the floor.
6. Claw marks score the walls in parallel lines -- something was dragged through here. Recently.
7. An overturned cart spills corroded tools across the passage. The wood has begun to sprout mushrooms.
8. Water drips from a crack in the ceiling into a shallow pool that reflects no light.
9. The petrified bark walls bear the faded ghost of an Arborist mural -- a tree in bloom, half-obscured by fungal growth.
10. A collapsed doorframe reveals a room beyond, sealed by a wall of solidified amber. Something is preserved inside.
11. Roots push through the ceiling in thick tangles, their surfaces slick with dark sap.
12. The floor is covered in a thin layer of fine glass-like shards -- the remnants of shattered amber.
13. An old campfire ring holds cold ashes and the bones of a small animal, picked clean.
14. The walls here are warm to the touch. An Aether vein runs behind the stone, pulsing faintly.
15. A Seeker's pack lies abandoned against the wall. The straps are cut, not unbuckled.
16. Shelf-fungi grow from the walls in shelf-like formations, their edges dripping a clear, viscous liquid.
17. The air here is noticeably colder. Frost patterns trace the stone where the Ashenmere Root-Road passes deep below.
18. An ironbark door stands intact in a ruined frame, its surface carved with a symbol no one in Emberhome recognizes.
19. The corridor narrows to a crawl-space. Scratches on the stone show where others have squeezed through -- and where some got stuck.
20. A broken Arborist mechanism lies scattered across the floor -- gears, amber lenses, and copper wire, all corroded beyond function.
21. The ceiling here is impossibly high, vanishing into darkness. Occasional sounds drift down from above -- clicks, shuffles, the scrape of chitin.
22. A row of empty alcoves lines the wall, each sized to hold a humanoid figure. Three of the six are stained with grey residue.
23. The stone floor is cracked in a radial pattern, as if something heavy landed here from a great height.
24. A thin stream of golden sap trickles down the wall, pooling in a natural basin. The liquid glows faintly.
25. Someone has scratched a message into the wall at knee height: "TURN BACK. THE SONG STOPS HERE."
26. An intact Arborist light-sconce flickers on the wall, its amber lens casting a warm circle on the opposite stone. Everything outside the circle is darker than it should be.
27. The passage branches around a pillar of petrified heartwood, its grain still visible under centuries of dust. Both paths look equally dangerous.
28. A colony of pale, blind beetles covers the lower walls, flowing around obstacles like living water. They avoid the light.
29. The floor here is sticky with dried rot-fluid. Bootprints preserved in the residue lead deeper, then stop.
30. An amber-inlaid bench stands against the wall, perfectly preserved amid the ruin. It looks like it was placed here yesterday.

### Random Events

Events trigger during exploration to keep the dungeon feeling alive. They carry no mechanical weight unless noted, but they set mood and foreshadow danger.

1. The DoomClock ticks forward -- distant rumbling shakes loose a rain of stone dust.
2. A rot-touched animal wanders past, ignoring the party. Its movements are mechanical, purposeless.
3. Ash rain begins falling from cracks in the ceiling, coating everything in a fine grey layer.
4. A distant scream echoes through the corridors. Human. Recent. Then silence.
5. The torches flicker and dim momentarily, as if something drew the warmth from the air.
6. The Root-Song shifts -- a pulse of urgency vibrates through the floor, then fades.
7. A Hollow shambles past a side corridor, carrying salvage in its arms. It does not notice the party.
8. The walls creak and groan as the Burnwillow thrashes in its sleep. A new crack splits the ceiling.
9. A faint amber glow pulses from behind a sealed wall. An Amber Vault is nearby.
10. The smell of woodsmoke reaches you from an impossible direction. Someone else is in the dungeon.
11. A Scrap Imp darts across the corridor clutching something shiny, chittering with glee.
12. The temperature drops sharply. Breath becomes visible. The cold lasts exactly ten heartbeats, then passes.
13. A section of floor gives way, revealing a lower passage. The drop is survivable. Probably.
14. An echo of the Root-Song carries a fragment of memory -- a glimpse of the tree in its Golden Age, green and whole.
15. A patrol of Charred Skeletons marches past in formation, following a route they walked in life. They do not deviate.
16. Spore-fall begins -- a slow drift of pale particles from fungal colonies above. The air tastes of copper.
17. The light from your torch catches something metallic embedded in the wall. A key? A trap mechanism? A relic?
18. A tremor shakes the corridor. When it stops, a previously sealed door stands slightly ajar.
19. The sound of running water comes from ahead -- an underground stream, or a ruptured Aether vein.
20. A Cinder Wolf howls in the distance. A second answers from a different direction. Then a third.
21. You find a Seeker's journal, its last entry dated three days ago: "Found the Vault. Can't open it. Something follows."
22. The fungi on the walls pulse in unison, brightening and dimming like a slow heartbeat.
23. A draft carries the scent of growing things -- impossible this deep. Fresh sap. Green leaves. It fades.
24. The stone beneath your feet vibrates with a rhythm that matches your heartbeat. When you stop, it stops.
25. A Blight-Hawk circles overhead in a vertical shaft, riding thermals. It watches you pass, but does not descend.

### Ambient Sounds

Roll or select from this table to establish the baseline audio layer for any room:

1. The slow drip of water on stone.
2. A distant, rhythmic tapping -- metal on rock.
3. The creak and groan of the Burnwillow settling.
4. Scuttling in the walls, just out of sight.
5. A subsonic hum that you feel more than hear.
6. The whisper of ash settling on a flat surface.
7. A faint, melodic sound -- the ghost of Root-Song.
8. The click-click-click of Clockwork Spider legs.
9. Something large breathing in the dark beyond the light.
10. Silence. Complete, oppressive, unnatural silence.

### Scavenge Finds

When a player searches a room, roll on this table for incidental discoveries. These are flavor items -- minor loot, crafting materials, or lore fragments.

1. A handful of corroded copper coins, worthless but historical.
2. A cracked amber chip containing a fragment of a child's laughter.
3. An Arborist tool -- function unknown, handle warm to the touch.
4. A Seeker's map fragment, partially accurate, partially wishful thinking.
5. A sealed glass vial containing clear liquid that glows faintly blue.
6. A bone trinket carved in the shape of a leaf, worn smooth by handling.
7. Iron nails, straight and usable -- valuable as crafting material.
8. A journal page describing a room that matches no known location.
9. A fungal growth that, when crushed, emits a sharp white light for ten seconds.
10. An amber bead on a leather cord -- Birsa would pay well for this.

### DoomClock Threshold Events

When the DoomClock reaches a threshold, the GM selects or rolls from the corresponding event pool. These events are not optional -- the dungeon is reacting to the party's presence.

**Threshold 5 -- Alert:**
- The torches flicker. Distant growls echo through the stone.
- Rot creatures in adjacent rooms become alert and begin patrolling.
- A faint ashfall begins drifting from the ceiling.
- The Root-Song carries a note of warning -- urgent, low, insistent.

**Threshold 10 -- The Hunt Begins:**
- Something stirs at the dungeon entrance. The hunt begins.
- The Rot Hunter spawns at the entry room and begins tracking the party.
- Corrupted NPCs in the zone become hostile.
- The air thickens with spores. Visibility drops.

**Threshold 13 -- Escalation:**
- Patrol routes shift. Every enemy in the zone is now actively searching.
- All enemy DC checks increase by +2.
- New hazards appear in previously safe rooms.
- A secondary entrance to the zone seals shut.

**Threshold 17 -- The Blight Spreads:**
- The Blight spreads visibly -- fungal growth covers walls in minutes.
- HP cannot be restored naturally. Healing items work at half effect.
- The Root-Song falls completely silent.
- Tier escalation: all enemies gain +2 to hit and +2 to damage.

**Threshold 22 -- Sealed:**
- The dungeon seals. No escape. Root-Road exits lock.
- Must fight to the zone boss or die trying.
- The DoomClock cannot be reduced below 17.
- All remaining Hollows in the zone converge on the party's position.

Quests are generated by combining four elements: a patron who gives the quest, an objective to accomplish, a complication that makes it harder, and a reward for completion. The GM can roll on each table independently or select elements that connect to the current narrative.

Warden Voss, Father Oaken, Scout Lira, Elder Maven, Keeper Elara, a desperate refugee, The Wanderer, Captain Ashara, Patchwork Pete, Old Miner Grod.

2. A second enemy faction arrives from an unexpected direction.
3. The target has already been corrupted by the Blight.
4. An NPC ally's secret is revealed at the worst possible moment.
5. The environment shifts mid-mission -- the Burnwillow thrashes.

Quests are tracked in the run state with the following structure: quest_id (unique identifier), title (display name), type (kill/fetch/escort/scout), objectives (list of conditions), rewards (list of items or effects), and turn-in NPC (who the player reports to on completion). Active quests persist within a run. Completed quests are logged in the meta-state for legacy tracking.

## Chapter 31: Solo Play

### Playing Alone

Burnwillow can be played solo -- one person acting as both GM and player. The procedural generation tables (Chapter 29) handle dungeon creation, and the dice handle enemy behavior. The solo player makes decisions for their characters and rolls for enemies using the targeting rules below. The game was built for this. The Doom Clock does not care how many people are at the table.

### Running Multiple Characters

Solo players should run 2-3 characters simultaneously. One character acts as the "leader" -- they make exploration decisions, talk to NPCs first, and choose which doors to open. But all characters act independently in combat. Rotate which character searches rooms, negotiates with merchants, and makes skill checks. This prevents one character from dominating the action economy and gives each build room to breathe. A party of a Frontline, a Ghost, and a Mender plays very differently from three Frontlines, even when the same person is rolling all the dice.

### Companions

The GM (or solo player) can add companion characters to the party using simplified personality profiles. Companions are not full characters -- they have stats and gear like any character, but their decisions follow simple heuristics:

| Companion Type | Combat Priority | Exploration Priority | Personality |
|---------------|----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Vanguard** | Attack the strongest enemy. Guard wounded allies. | Move forward. Kick doors. | Battle-hardened, few words, leads by example. |
| **Scholar** | Target enemies weak to Aether. Use trait abilities first. | Search every room. Examine everything. | Curious, cautious, narrates observations. |
| **Scavenger** | Finish off wounded enemies (for loot). Avoid damage. | Loot first, ask questions never. | Opportunistic, fast-talking, always pocketing things. |
| **Healer** | Triage the most wounded ally. Stay out of melee. | Check for hazards. Warn the party. | Calm, careful, speaks softly, counts supplies. |

### Companion Decisions

When it is a companion's turn, ask: "What would this personality do?" If unsure, roll 1d6:

- **1-2:** Cautious action (defend, heal, retreat)
- **3-4:** Standard action (attack nearest, search, move forward)
- **5-6:** Bold action (charge strongest, use trait ability, explore unknown exit)

### Enemy Targeting (Solo)

When enemies attack, roll 1d6 per enemy to determine their target. Assign numbers to party members (1-2: Character A, 3-4: Character B, 5-6: Character C). Enemies preferentially target wounded characters -- if a character is below half HP, they are targeted on a 1-4 instead of their normal range.

### Solo Doom Clock

For solo play with fewer than 3 characters, advance the Doom Clock by 1 every 2 rooms instead of every room. The pressure should still build, but a smaller party needs more time to clear rooms without being overwhelmed. With 3 characters, use the standard Doom Clock advancement rate.

### Solo Difficulty

| Party Size | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| **1 character** | Reduce enemy count by 1 per room (minimum 1). Start with a companion. The Doom Clock advances every 2 rooms. |
| **2 characters** | Standard enemy count. Doom Clock advances every 2 rooms. |
| **3 characters** | Standard rules. Solo play at its best balance. |

### Solo Campaign Structure

Solo campaigns tend to be shorter -- 5-8 sessions -- and more personal. Focus on one faction relationship rather than spreading across all six. Let the companion's personality create drama: a Scavenger companion who loots a sacred Hive item creates faction tension the solo player must resolve. A Vanguard companion who kicks open a door the party was not ready for forces improvisation. The companion is not a tool. It is a co-author of the story, driven by a personality that sometimes conflicts with the player's plans. That friction is the game.

## Chapter 32: Meta-Progression

The Burnwillow is a roguelite. Characters die permanently. But the world remembers.

The save state is divided into two layers: **run_state** (ephemeral, wiped on death) and **meta_state** (persistent, survives death). This division is the mechanical expression of the game's central theme: individual lives are temporary, but the community endures.

### Run State (Ephemeral)

The run state tracks everything about the current character and their run:

- **HP / Max HP:** Current and maximum hit points. Max HP = 10 + Grit Modifier (see Chapter 6).
- **Stats:** The Four Pillars -- Might, Wits, Grit, Aether. Each stat has a score (3-18, rolled 4d6 drop lowest) and a derived modifier.
- **GearGrid:** The 10-slot equipment system (see Chapter 14). Each slot is either null (empty) or contains an item.
- **Inventory:** Backpack items -- potions, keys, scrolls, Memory Seeds.
- **Dungeon Depth:** Current floor number (0 = surface/town).
- **Doom Clock:** Current DoomClock value. Starts at 0 each run.
- **Character Name:** Randomly generated or player-set. Maximum 32 characters.
- **Run Statistics:** Floors cleared, enemies slain, turns taken, chests opened, keys used, scrolls read.
- **Timestamp Born:** ISO 8601 timestamp of character creation.

When a character dies, the entire run state is wiped. The GearGrid empties. The stats vanish. The DoomClock resets. You start over. This is not punishment. It is the cycle.

### Meta State (Persistent)

The meta state tracks everything that survives death:

- **Legacy Currency:** Burnwillow Sap or similar meta-resource, accumulated across runs.
- **Unlocked Blueprints:** Item blueprints discovered via Memory Seeds. Persist across deaths. Per-save scoping (see Chapter 26).
- **Town Status:** Building upgrade levels for Emberhome's four structures (Blacksmith, Alchemist, Temple, Library). Each ranges from 0 to 5 (see Chapter 18).
- **Total Deaths:** A running count of fallen heroes.
- **Deepest Floor Reached:** The furthest anyone has delved. A high-water mark.
- **Graveyard:** A memorial array for past characters.

### The Graveyard

Every character who dies is added to the graveyard. Each entry records:

| Field | Type | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| name | string | The fallen character's name |
| death_floor | integer | The floor where they fell |
| timestamp | datetime | When they died |
| epitaph | string | A short memorial (max 128 characters) |

The graveyard is not a scoreboard. It is a memorial wall. The Chapel of the Eternal Flame bears the carved names of every Seeker who never returned (see Chapter 18). The graveyard is the mechanical implementation of that fiction. Characters who die in the Rot Marsh at level 1 sit beside characters who fell fighting the Clockwork Archon at the bottom of the Rot-Heart. The tree does not rank its dead.

### Run Statistics

Each life tracks the following performance metrics:

| Statistic | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| floors_cleared | Number of dungeon floors completed |
| enemies_slain | Total enemies killed this run |
| turns_taken | Total actions taken |
| chests_opened | Treasure chests looted |
| keys_used | Keys consumed |
| scrolls_read | Scrolls activated |

These statistics serve two purposes: they provide the player with a post-death summary that contextualizes how the run went, and they feed into the meta-state's legacy tracking. A character who cleared seven floors and slain forty enemies before falling to the Blight Mother is remembered differently than one who died to a Rot-Beetle on floor one. Both are recorded. Both matter.

If your Total Modifier + 1 meets or exceeds the DC, you succeed automatically without rolling. Failure is mathematically impossible given your current equipment. The GM may still call for a roll on dramatic moments, but the outcome is guaranteed.

## Appendix B: Glossary

**Aether** -- The golden sap of the Burnwillow. The tree's circulatory fluid and magical substrate. Powers all Arborist technology. One of the four core stats.

**Amber** -- Solidified Aether, hardened around moments of significance. Currency, crafting material, sacred relic, and lore delivery mechanism. Every shard holds a memory.

**Arborist** -- Ancient order of tree-shapers who tended all four Groves during the Golden Age. They sang wood into shape. They are gone. Their relics remain.

**Blight** -- See Rot.

**Doom Clock** -- A pressure system that tracks the party's cumulative impact on the dungeon ecosystem. Starts at 0 each run. Advances with room transitions, combat noise, and failed checks. Thresholds at 5, 10, 13, 17, and 22 trigger escalating consequences. Not a timer -- a thermometer.

**GearGrid** -- The 10-slot equipment system. Head, Shoulders, Chest, Arms, Legs, R.Hand, L.Hand, R.Ring, L.Ring, Neck. Gear defines character power. There are no levels. There are no classes. You are what you wear.

**Gear Tier** -- The material quality of an item. Tier I (Scrap/Wood) through Tier IV (Ambercore/Sunresin). Each tier adds +1d6 to the relevant stat pool. The engine implements Tiers I-IV.

**Grit** -- One of the four core stats. Fortitude, HP, stamina. Max HP = 10 + Grit Modifier.

**Hollow** -- A creature or person consumed by the Blight. Mindless husks that retain physical capability and muscle memory. They do not speak, sleep, or retreat. Fallen Seekers become Hollows.

**Kindler** -- One of two Emberhome vocations. Kindlers tend the village and the Hearth. They do not delve.

**Memory Seed** -- Fossilized Arborist crafting blueprint. Found in Amber Vaults. Must be carried to Emberhome to unlock a permanent recipe. Per-save scoping -- each campaign has its own unlock history.

**Might** -- One of the four core stats. Physical power, melee combat, feats of strength.

**Root-Song** -- The Burnwillow's subsonic communication. Felt, not heard. A vibration in the floor and the base of the skull. Characters with Aether 14+ can interpret fragments. Falls silent where the Rot spreads.

**Rot** -- Parasitic fungal corruption rising from the deepest root layers. Not a creature, disease, or army. A process -- entropy given biological form. Cold, grey, silent. Spreads through moisture, darkness, and stillness. Also called the Blight.

**Rot Hunter** -- Apex Hollow. Spawns at Doom 10 at the dungeon entry room. BFS pursuit -- always moves toward the party. Cannot be killed with Tier I-II weapons (DR 3). The intended response when it appears is to run.

**Seeker** -- One of two Emberhome vocations. Seekers delve into the Burnwillow's depths. They are the player characters.

**Sun-Fruit** -- Golden fruit that grows exclusively in the Crown. Cures Blight, heals 2d6+2 HP, reduces Doom Clock by 1, recharges 1 Implement charge. Harvesting is lethal.

**Wits** -- One of the four core stats. Mental acuity, ranged combat, mechanisms, perception. Defense = 10 + Wits Modifier.

## Appendix C: Design Pillars & Open Questions

### The Nine Design Pillars

1. **Druidic, not industrial.** Shamanic rituals, root-song, sap-reading. Arborists sang wood into shape. Not factories, not machines, not biopunk.

2. **The tree IS the world.** Not a planet with a big tree. The canopy is the sky. The roots are the underworld. There is nothing else.

3. **You are what you wear.** No XP, no leveling. Gear tier is character power. Equipment defines the class.

4. **Death means something.** Permanent death. Characters become Hollows. Emberhome persists. Legacy travels via Heirlooms and Memory Seeds.

5. **The clock is always ticking.** Doom Clock creates urgency. Every action costs the world something.

6. **Metroidvania gating.** Players see locked Vaults early. Come back with tools to open them. The Sun-Bow behind unbreakable amber.

7. **Memory Seeds as legacy.** Per-save recipe unlocks give each campaign a distinct development arc.

8. **NPCs have history.** Persistent memory, cultural bias, faction relationships. The world reacts to what the players have done.

9. **Each season is a complete reality.** Four Groves, four campaigns, four tonal identities -- connected by Root-Roads.

### Open Questions

These are unresolved design questions that remain under active consideration:

- **The First Druids** -- Who were they? What happened? Why were the Root-Roads sealed?
- **Root-Road Discovery** -- How are Root-Roads discovered in gameplay? Zone 4 discovery? Quest chain? Dowsing Rod at maximum range?
- **Dead Groves** -- Are there fifth or sixth trees that have already fallen entirely?
- **Heirloom Mechanics** -- How are items designated as heirlooms before death? What is the handoff ritual?
- **The Arborist Singing Tradition** -- Can players learn fragments of it? Purchasable skill or discovered ability?
- **Twitch's Communication Crystal** -- What does it connect to? A surviving Arborist? Ashenmere? The Root-Roads themselves?
- **Inter-Faction Politics** -- Do equivalents of the Six Factions exist in Verdhollow, Solheart, Ashenmere?
- **Faction Zone Presence** -- Which factions control which dungeon territory? How does reputation affect encounters?
- **Alchemy Ingredient Ecology** -- Do ingredients respawn? Are they seasonal? Can they be cultivated in Emberhome?
- **The Rot's Origin** -- Ashknot's discovery that the Rot follows Root-Road infrastructure implies deliberate introduction. From which Grove? By whom?

## Chapter 33: Running a Campaign

Burnwillow campaigns range from a single evening's delve to a dozen sessions that carry characters from Emberhome's threshold to the Crown of the world-tree. The structure is always the same: the settlement is safe, the dungeon is not, and the Doom Clock never stops.

### Session Zero

Before the first delve, sit down with your players and establish:

- **Tone.** Burnwillow is druidic horror, not heroic fantasy. Characters die. The tree is dying. Hope exists, but it is earned, not given. Make sure everyone is comfortable with permanent death and the slow-burn tension of the Doom Clock.
- **Party size.** 2-4 characters is ideal. Solo play works but is brutally lethal. Five or more characters dilute the scarcity that drives the game.
- **Campaign length.** Decide in advance:

| Format | Sessions | Scope |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **One-Shot** | 1 | Single delve, Zones 1-2, one boss. Pre-built characters recommended. |
| **Short Campaign** | 3-5 | Zones 1-3, one faction arc, one major quest line. |
| **Standard Campaign** | 8-12 | Full Zones 1-5, multiple factions, town upgrades, legacy characters. |
| **Open-Ended** | Ongoing | Sandbox. Players set their own goals. The tree keeps dying. |

- **The Prologue.** Run a collaborative world-building exercise at the table. The GM describes Emberhome. Each player contributes one detail: a sound they hear, a smell, a person they recognize, a rumor they've heard. By the time the exercise is done, the settlement feels like it belongs to everyone -- not just the GM.

### Session Structure

A typical Burnwillow session follows a four-beat rhythm:

**1. Emberhome (15-20 minutes)**
The party is in the settlement. They rest, heal, trade with NPCs, craft at the forge or alchemy bench, upgrade equipment, check the bounty board, and talk to faction contacts. The Doom Clock does not advance in Emberhome. This is the only safe ground in the game. Let players breathe here -- the dungeon will not let them.

**2. The Delve (60-90 minutes)**
The party descends into the Burnwillow's roots. The GM generates rooms using the tables in Chapter 29 or a pre-built module. Each room entered advances the Doom Clock by 1. Players explore, fight, search, and make hard choices about how much time they can afford to spend. The tension should build steadily -- early rooms are manageable, but the Doom Clock creates mounting pressure.

**3. The Climax (20-30 minutes)**
The delve reaches its crisis point: a boss encounter, a Rot Hunter closing in, a locked Vault that demands a choice, or the Doom Clock hitting a threshold that forces a retreat. This is where sessions become memorable. The climax should feel like a consequence of every room the party chose to enter and every resource they chose to spend.

**4. The Aftermath (10-15 minutes)**
The party escapes or dies. Survivors return to Emberhome with loot, stories, and scars. Dead characters are memorialized -- their names go on the Chapel wall, their gear becomes salvage, and their Memory Seeds pass to the next generation. Town upgrades are applied. The GM notes what changed in the world. The Doom Clock resets for the next delve (but the tree remembers).

### Campaign Arc Pacing

For a standard 8-12 session campaign, the arc follows the dungeon Zones:

| Sessions | Zone | Gear Tier | What Happens |
|----------|------|-----------|-------------|
| 1-2 | Zone 1: The Tangle | I-II | Learn the basics. Meet Emberhome NPCs. First faction contact. First character death is likely. |
| 3-4 | Zone 2: Stone-Grip | II-III | Faction reputation building. The Doom Clock starts to feel real. Crafting stations unlock. Gear builds start to take shape. |
| 5-7 | Zone 3: Aether-Flow | III | Faction quest lines reach their midpoints. Gear sets begin to come together. Hard choices between opposing factions. The Rot Hunter appears. |
| 8-10 | Zone 4: Rot-Heart | III-IV | High lethality. Faction climaxes. The Root-Song goes silent. Characters are defined by their builds. |
| 11-12 | Zone 5: The Crown | IV | Endgame. Sun-Fruits. The final boss. Legacy. The tree's fate is decided -- or at least delayed. |

**For a one-shot:** Start at Zone 2 with pre-built Tier II characters. Set the Doom Clock to 5. Place one boss at the end. Give each player a faction contact and a personal goal. Play for 3-4 hours.

### Dungeon Generation at the Table

When the party enters a new room, the GM rolls on these tables (see Chapter 29 for full tables):

1. **Room Type:** Roll 1d6. 1-3: Normal. 4: Treasure. 5: Hazard. 6: Special (secret, return gate, NPC encounter).
2. **Room Description:** Roll 1d8 on the tier-appropriate room description table (see Chapter 20).
3. **Enemies:** Roll 1d6. 1-2: Empty. 3-4: 1d3 tier-appropriate enemies. 5: 1d3+1 enemies. 6: Elite or mini-boss.
4. **Loot:** Roll 1d6. 1-3: Nothing. 4-5: One tier-appropriate item. 6: One item from the next tier up.
5. **Hazard:** If the room has a hazard, roll on the tier-appropriate hazard table (see Chapter 28).
6. **Connections:** The room has 1d3+1 exits (cardinal directions). One always leads deeper. One may loop back.

The GM can pre-generate a dungeon floor before the session or roll live at the table. Pre-generation is faster; live rolling is more surprising for everyone, including the GM.

### Faction Reputation at the Table

Track faction reputation on the GM's notes or a shared faction sheet. Each faction starts at Neutral (0). Record changes as they happen:

- **Quest completed for a faction:** +1 reputation
- **Gift or favor to a faction:** +1 reputation (once per session per faction)
- **Killed a faction member:** -2 reputation
- **Sided against a faction in a dispute:** -1 reputation
- **Reached a Doom threshold while in faction territory:** -1 (the faction blames the party for drawing danger)

Remind players of opposing faction tensions (see Chapter 5). When they raise reputation with one side of an opposing pair, lower the other by 1. Players will have to choose. That is the point.

### NPC Roleplaying

Every named NPC in this book (see Chapter 22) has a **Voice** line -- a one-sentence prompt for how they speak. Use it. You do not need to do a full voice performance. The Voice line tells you the rhythm, the vocabulary, and the emotional register. That is enough.

- **Elder Kaelen:** "Precise, unhurried. Never uses contractions when discussing the Blight. 'It does not stop. We do.'"
- **Twitch:** "Rapid, associative, prone to trailing off. Finishes sentences with questions. 'You know what I mean? Do you?'"
- **Brindlemaw:** "Ancient, dry, speaks in absolute certainties. Never raises her voice. Does not need to."

When a player talks to an NPC, ask yourself: what does this NPC **want** and what do they **need**? The want is what they say. The need is what they reveal when the player earns their trust. Every NPC interaction is a negotiation between surface and depth.

### The Doom Clock as a Physical Object

The Doom Clock should be visible to all players at all times. Use a d20 set to the current value, or mark a track on paper from 0 to 22. When the GM advances the Doom Clock, they should:

1. Pick up the die (or pencil) visibly.
2. State the new value.
3. If a threshold was crossed, read the threshold text aloud (see Appendix A).

The Doom Clock is the heartbeat of the game. It should never be invisible, never be forgotten, and never be advanced silently. Players should feel every tick.

### Difficulty Scaling

| Situation | Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Fewer than 3 players** | Reduce enemy count per room by 1 (minimum 1). Do not reduce enemy stats. |
| **New players** | Start with pre-built characters. Use Routine DC (8) for the first session. Introduce Hard DC (12) in session 2. |
| **Experienced players** | Add one environmental hazard to every room. Start the Doom Clock at 3 instead of 0. |
| **Too easy** | Increase enemy tier by 1 in Zones the party has already cleared. The Rot adapts. |
| **Too lethal** | Allow one "Ember's Grace" per session: when a character would die, they instead drop to 1 HP and the Doom Clock advances by 3. |

### Between Sessions

- **Characters persist.** Gear, stats, inventory, and faction reputation carry over.
- **Town upgrades carry over.** The Blacksmith the party invested in stays upgraded.
- **Dead characters become Hollows.** The GM can reintroduce them as dungeon encounters. This is devastating and should be used sparingly.
- **Memory Seeds persist.** New characters created by the same player benefit from recipes unlocked by previous characters. Death has meaning because something survives it.
- **The world changes.** If the party completed a faction quest, the faction's situation improves. If they failed, it worsens. The GM should open each session with one sentence about what changed in Emberhome since last time.
